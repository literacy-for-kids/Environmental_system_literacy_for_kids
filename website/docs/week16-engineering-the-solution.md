# Week 16: Specifications, Constraints, and Tradeoffs
*Unit 5: The Capstone — From Sketch to Proposal*

Last week the proposal got drafted. This week it gets sharper.

The Week 15 proposal said *what* the redesign was. This week's work specifies *exactly* what success looks like, *exactly* what it costs, and *exactly* what gets traded away to make the loop close. Real engineering documents don't just describe — they specify. Specifications are measurable. Constraints are named. Tradeoffs are explicit.

The most important concept this week is the **minimum viable loop**: the smallest version of the redesign that still closes the loop. A perfect proposal that never gets implemented does less for the system than a minimum viable version that's actually possible. The student's job this week is to find the version of their redesign that is both *real* and *small enough to actually happen*.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- The transition this week is from vague ("we'll compost") to specific ("we'll compost 4 kg/week of fruit and vegetable scraps in a 60-liter backyard bin emptied to garden beds every spring").
- Constraints should be named honestly. Every redesign costs something. The proposal is more credible when the costs are visible.
- The minimum viable loop is not a compromise — it's an engineering choice. The smallest working version often beats the perfect version on paper.
- By end of week, the student should have a one-page Specs Sheet ready for the stress-test in Week 17.
:::

## Facilitator Preparation

:::info Before You Begin
- Pull last week's proposal draft. The Specs Sheet builds directly on it.
- Have a sample Specs Sheet structure ready (provided in Session 1).
- Be willing to push back on hand-wavy specifications. "Less waste" is not a spec. "≤ 1 kg/week of food waste to landfill, down from 4 kg/week" is a spec.
- Have the Systems Log handy.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
**A spec is something you could later measure.** If the student writes "improve waste handling," ask: "How will we know if this happened? What number would we look at six months from now?"

**Constraints are not bad news.** Honest constraints make the proposal stronger, not weaker. A redesign that pretends to be free is a redesign that won't survive contact with reality.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "We're going to make our plan more specific. Instead of 'we'll compost,' we'll write down *exactly* how much, *exactly* where, and *exactly* how often. We'll also write down what's hard about it."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip the hard/soft constraint distinction. Just list "things that will be tricky."
- Skip the formal Specs Sheet format. Use a simple three-column chart: "What we'll do" / "How we'll know it's working" / "What's hard about it."

**Adapting the activities:**
- Help with quantities. Use real measurements together — "let's see how many banana peels we make in one day, then multiply by 7."
- Use real objects for space — "the compost bin would be about this big," pointing to a comparable household object.

**Journal alternatives:** A drawing of the redesigned process with sticky-note labels: "this much," "this often," "this tricky."

**What success looks like:** The learner can answer "how much, how often, where, what's hard" about their redesign.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Require at least three quantitative specs (waste diverted, frequency, volume, cost, time, etc.).
- Make the hard/soft constraint distinction explicit. A hard constraint is one you cannot work around (a 600 sq ft apartment cannot host a 6 ft compost bin). A soft constraint is one you could reduce with effort (the family might not separate scraps consistently — but training and labeling could improve compliance).
- Ask: "What's the *cheapest* version of this redesign that still closes the loop?"
:::

## Guided Session 1
### Writing Specs
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- convert a vague proposal into specific, measurable specifications
- define success in concrete, measurable terms
- identify the minimum viable version of the redesign

---

#### Activities

**1. The Specification Test**

Read the Week 15 proposal out loud. As each sentence is read, ask:

> "Is that specific? Could I measure that? If we did this for six months, how would we know whether it worked?"

If the answer is "I'm not sure," the line needs to become a spec.

Examples of converting vague to specific:

| Vague | Specific |
|---|---|
| "We'll compost food scraps." | "Approximately 4 kg/week of fruit and vegetable scraps will be deposited in a 60-liter outdoor compost bin." |
| "The bin will be emptied regularly." | "The kitchen pail (5L) will be emptied into the outdoor bin twice per week." |
| "Compost will be used in the garden." | "Approximately 30 kg of finished compost per year will be applied to vegetable beds in spring and fall." |
| "We'll save money on trash." | "Household landfill-bound waste will drop by ~50% by weight." |
| "The family will help." | "Each family member will deposit fruit and vegetable scraps in the kitchen pail at point-of-use; one designated person will manage the outdoor bin weekly." |

The right column is harder to write but easier to act on.

---

**2. The Specs Sheet Template**

Here's the structure the student fills out:

```
SPECS SHEET — [PROPOSAL TITLE]

INPUTS (precise)
  Materials in: _____ (quantity, frequency, source)
  Energy in: _____
  Labor in: _____ minutes per week

OUTPUTS (precise)
  Intended output: _____ (quantity, frequency)
  Recovered material: _____ (quantity, frequency)
  Residual waste: _____ (quantity, frequency, destination)

SUCCESS METRICS
  Metric 1: _____ (e.g., kg of food waste diverted from landfill per month)
    Target value: _____
  Metric 2: _____ (e.g., % of household members participating)
    Target value: _____
  Metric 3: _____ (e.g., volume of finished compost produced per year)
    Target value: _____

OPERATING PARAMETERS
  Frequency of routine actions: _____
  Maintenance interval: _____
  Failure mode (what happens if it gets skipped for a week): _____
```

The Specs Sheet should fit on one page. If it spills over, the redesign is probably too big.

---

**3. Define Success — In Numbers**

The most important line on the sheet is the **success metric**. The student writes one sentence:

> "This redesign succeeds if, after six months, ____________________."

Examples:

- "...the household sends ≤ 2 kg/week of food waste to landfill (down from ~4 kg)."
- "...at least 50% of paper handouts from school are reused or composted, instead of trashed."
- "...the household uses ≤ 5 disposable plastic water bottles per month (down from ~30)."

If the student can't write this sentence cleanly, the spec isn't ready yet.

---

**4. The Minimum Viable Loop**

Now the engineering question: **what is the smallest version of this redesign that still closes the loop?**

The student writes a second version of the Specs Sheet — the MVL version. The rule: strip out anything that isn't essential to closing the loop. Keep only what's required.

For the composting example:

| Full version | Minimum viable version |
|---|---|
| Backyard compost bin, ~$60, 60L capacity | Freezer-bag collection, drop at community compost site weekly, $0 |
| All food scraps including dairy/meat | Fruit and vegetable scraps only (simpler, no smell management) |
| Garden application twice per year | Hand off finished compost to neighbor with garden, no garden required |
| All 4 family members participating | One family member operates the system, others just put scraps in the bin |

The MVL might lose some benefits (less compost produced, fewer family members building the habit) — but it's *real*. It can start tomorrow. The full version may take six months to set up. The MVL takes a week.

> "A perfect solution that never gets implemented does nothing for the system. A minimum viable version that's actually possible closes the loop today."

The student picks: full version, MVL, or something in between. Whichever they pick, that's the version that goes through stress-testing in Week 17.

## Guided Session 2
### Constraints and Tradeoffs
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- identify the constraints on their redesign across five standard categories
- distinguish hard constraints (cannot be worked around) from soft constraints (can be reduced)
- name explicit tradeoffs the redesign introduces

---

#### Activities

**1. The Five Constraint Categories**

Every redesign runs into constraints. The student walks through all five categories:

**Cost.** What does the redesign actually cost to implement?
- Initial cost (one-time materials, setup labor)
- Ongoing cost (consumables, time, energy)
- Hidden cost (replacement parts, training, education)

**Space.** Where does the new component physically go?
- Outdoor space requirement
- Indoor space requirement
- Storage for collected materials between cycles

**Time.** How much extra time per week does this take?
- Setup time (one-time)
- Per-week ongoing time
- Periodic maintenance time

**Behavior.** Who needs to change what habits?
- New habits required (sorting at point-of-use, etc.)
- Old habits to drop (throwing things in the wrong bin)
- Training required for new participants

**Cooperation.** Who else needs to participate, and at what level?
- Active participants (must do things)
- Passive participants (must not block the system)
- External participants (community composters, neighbors, etc.)

The student writes a paragraph for each category, specific to their redesign.

---

**2. Hard vs. Soft Constraints**

For each constraint identified, the student labels it:

- **Hard constraint** — cannot be worked around within the bounds of the project. Example: a small apartment cannot host an outdoor compost bin.
- **Soft constraint** — can be reduced with effort or different design choices. Example: family member compliance can be improved with clear labeling, weekly reminders, or making the new bin more convenient than the trash bin.

Why this distinction matters: **hard constraints define the bounds of what's possible.** If a hard constraint kills the redesign, the redesign needs to be re-scoped or replaced. Soft constraints just require effort to manage.

Many students will discover that what they thought was a hard constraint is actually soft — and vice versa.

---

**3. Explicit Tradeoffs**

Now the student writes out the tradeoffs explicitly, in the form:

> "We gain ____________________ but pay ____________________."

Examples:

- "We gain ~200 kg/year of organic matter returning to soil, but pay ~5 minutes per week of management time and ~$80 in initial bin costs."
- "We gain ~30 disposable bottles/month not going to landfill, but pay ~$50 for refillable bottles and ~10 minutes/day to clean and refill them."
- "We gain a closed-loop paper system at school, but pay roughly 15 minutes per week of teacher coordination time, plus the inconvenience of returning unfinished paper to a centralized bin instead of tossing it."

The "but pay" half is essential. A proposal that doesn't name what it costs isn't engineering. It's marketing.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Create the one-page Specs Sheet — the document that goes into stress-testing in Week 17.

### Activities

**1. Finalize the One-Page Specs Sheet**

The student combines everything from Sessions 1 and 2 onto a single page:

```
SPECS SHEET — [PROPOSAL TITLE]                            VERSION: 1.0

THE LOOP
  Current open-loop process: _____
  Proposed closed-loop redesign: _____
  Selected version: [Full / Minimum Viable / Hybrid]

SPECIFICATIONS
  Inputs: _____
  Outputs: _____
  Operating parameters: _____

SUCCESS METRICS
  Primary metric: _____ (target: _____)
  Secondary metric: _____ (target: _____)

CONSTRAINTS
  Cost: _____ (hard / soft)
  Space: _____ (hard / soft)
  Time: _____ (hard / soft)
  Behavior: _____ (hard / soft)
  Cooperation: _____ (hard / soft)

TRADEOFFS
  Gain: _____
  Pay: _____
```

One page. Specific numbers. Honest labels. This document is the heart of the capstone.

---

**2. The Reality Check Test**

The student reads the Specs Sheet to one other person — family member, friend, anyone. Then asks:

- "Does the proposal make sense as described?"
- "Can you tell what success would look like?"
- "What part seems most unrealistic to you?"
- "What did I leave out?"

Note the responses. They'll inform Week 17's stress-test.

---

**3. The Tradeoff Statement**

In the Systems Log, write the tradeoff statement as a single clean sentence:

> "My redesign trades [X cost or constraint] in exchange for [Y benefit to the system]."

This sentence is what the student will say when someone asks "so what does this thing cost?" Having it written down, in advance, means they can answer cleanly.

---

**4. Terrarium Check**

Week 13 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants — any changes?
- Notice: the terrarium has no Specs Sheet. The loop closes itself because biology has been doing this for billions of years. Engineered closed loops have to be designed explicitly. That's why a Specs Sheet matters.

### Systems Log

> **Specs Sheet (attached / pasted below):** ____
>
> **Primary success metric:** ____
>
> **Hardest constraint:** ____ (hard or soft?)
>
> **Tradeoff statement:** "My redesign trades ___ for ___."
>
> **Selected version:** Full / Minimum Viable / Hybrid
>
> **What I learned from the Reality Check:** ____
>
> **Terrarium update:** ____

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "My plan will work if ___ happens for ___ weeks."
- "The hardest part will be ___."
- "We gain ___ but it costs us ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Which version did you choose — full, minimum viable, or hybrid? What made you pick that one?
- What's one thing you almost left off the constraints list because it felt embarrassing or inconvenient to admit?
- If the redesign costs more than you expected, does that change anything about whether you want to implement it?

---

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Name a measurable success metric:** "How will you know in six months if your redesign worked?" (Looking for: a specific number or quantity, with a target.)
2. **Distinguish a hard constraint from a soft one:** "Pick one constraint on your redesign. Is it hard or soft, and how do you know?" (Looking for: a clear test — could you work around it with effort, or is it a fixed limit?)
3. **State the tradeoff:** "What does your redesign cost, and what does it produce?" (Looking for: an explicit "we gain X but pay Y" structure.)

If the learner can do all 3, the Specs Sheet is ready for stress-testing.

---

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Notice the difference between Week 15's proposal and this week's Specs Sheet. Last week you said 'we'll compost.' This week you said '4 kg/week, 60L bin, $80 initial cost, 5 minutes per week ongoing.' Which one would you trust more if you were the person reviewing the proposal?"

The Specs Sheet is more believable because it's more specific. Specific claims can be checked. Vague claims can't. That's why specifications matter — not because numbers are inherently good, but because numbers are accountable.

> "Also notice: by the time you wrote the Specs Sheet, you knew exactly what the redesign costs. The constraints are visible. The tradeoffs are named. There's no hidden bill that shows up later."

**This week's takeaway:** Specifications make a redesign accountable. Honest constraints make it credible. The smallest version that closes the loop is often the best version, because it's the one that actually happens.
:::

:::tip Simplify (Ages 8–9)
Three numbers are enough: how much waste reduced per week, how much money it costs, how much extra time per week. Plus one sentence: "The hardest part will be ___." That's a complete spec for a young learner.
:::

:::tip Extend (Ages 10–12)
Calculate the *cost per kg of waste diverted*. Take your total annual cost (initial bin + maintenance) divided by the kg of waste you'd divert per year. This is the kind of number a city budget officer would calculate for a municipal composting program. How does your household-level number compare to typical municipal costs (~$50–150 per ton)?
