# Week 15: Turning Your Redesign Into a Plan
*Unit 5: The Capstone — From Sketch to Proposal*

This week begins the capstone. Over the next four weeks, the student takes one open-loop process from their own life and develops it all the way from diagnosis to a real proposal presented to a real audience.

Week 15 is the formal diagnosis. The student maps the open-loop process in engineering terms: inputs, transformation, outputs, waste stream, environmental load. Then they begin drafting a structured proposal — not an essay, not a persuasion piece, but a proposal. The kind of document an engineer would write to recommend a system change.

The framing matters. This is not a school project to "raise awareness." It is an engineering proposal to redesign a specific process. The voice should sound like: *"The current configuration generates X kg of waste per year. A circular redesign would reduce this by Y% by routing the waste output to Z process."*

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- The student is committing to a single redesign topic for the next four weeks. Help them pick something tractable.
- The diagnosis must include concrete numbers, even if estimated. "A lot of food waste" is not a diagnosis. "About 4 kg of food scraps per week" is.
- The proposal draft is a first draft. It does not need to be polished. It needs to be honest about what the circular loop requires.
- If the student already did a redesign sketch in Week 14, they can build on it. If they want to switch topics, that's fine — but they need to commit by the end of this week.
:::

## Facilitator Preparation

:::info Before You Begin
- Pull the Systems Log and the student's Week 14 redesign sketch (if they completed one).
- Have a few sample proposal structures in mind — short, clean, organized.
- Be ready to push back if the diagnosis is vague. "How many kg per week?" "Where does it actually go after it leaves the house?"
- If the student wants to change topics from Week 14, that's allowed — but help them commit by end of session.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
**The diagnosis is the foundation.** If the student can't write a clean, specific diagnosis with real numbers, the rest of the capstone will float. Take time to make this section precise.

**The proposal voice is engineering, not advocacy.** Watch for and gently redirect language like "we have to save the planet" or "this is so wasteful." The voice should be neutral, specific, technical.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "We're going to write a plan to fix one thing. First we'll draw a picture of how it works now. Then we'll write down what we'd change and what we'd need to make it happen."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip exact measurements. "A lot," "some," and "a little" are fine, paired with rough counts ("about 5 banana peels a day").
- Skip the formal proposal sections. Use a simple three-part version: "What's happening now," "What we'd change," "What we'd need."
- Use drawings instead of written sections wherever possible.

**Adapting the activities:**
- Walk through the box-and-arrow diagram together. Help label each box.
- The proposal can be a one-page drawing with three sentences.

**Journal alternatives:** Two drawings (current process / new process) plus three sentences: "Right now, ___. We could change it by ___. To do this, we'd need ___."

**What success looks like:** The learner can describe their chosen process, draw what's happening, and name one specific change.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Push for numbers. Even rough estimates count: "about 30 plastic water bottles per month."
- Require all four proposal sections (Problem, Solution, Resources, Outcomes).
- Ask for at least one quantitative outcome target: "this would reduce X by approximately Y%."
:::

## Guided Session 1
### The System Diagnosis
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- formally diagnose an open-loop process using a box-and-arrow diagram
- identify the inputs, transformation, outputs, and waste outputs of the process
- estimate the environmental load (energy, materials, waste volume) in concrete units

---

#### Activities

**1. Lock In the Topic**

Have the student write at the top of a fresh page in the Systems Log:

```
CAPSTONE TOPIC: _______________________________
COMMITTED ON: [today's date]
```

Once written, the topic is locked for the next four weeks. (They can still refine the *scope* — narrow or broaden — but the underlying process stays.)

If the student is stuck, the strongest candidates are:

- The Week 14 redesign topic, scaled up slightly
- A process from their "Away" Audit in Week 7 (food waste, packaging, e-waste, paper)
- A specific household or school routine with a clear waste stream

---

**2. The Box-and-Arrow Diagram**

On a fresh sheet of paper, the student draws a box-and-arrow diagram of the current process. Format:

```
[INPUTS]  →  [TRANSFORMATION]  →  [INTENDED OUTPUT]
                                ↓
                          [WASTE OUTPUTS]  →  [WHERE WASTE GOES]
```

Each box gets labeled. Each arrow gets labeled with what flows along it. Example, for a household food-scrap process:

```
[Groceries: ~10 kg/week]  →  [Cooking + Eating]  →  [Meals eaten]
                                                 ↓
                                           [~4 kg food scraps/week]
                                                 ↓
                                           [Trash bag]
                                                 ↓
                                           [Curbside pickup]
                                                 ↓
                                           [Municipal landfill]
                                                 ↓
                                  [Anaerobic decomposition → methane emissions]
```

The point of the diagram is to make every step visible — including the steps that usually get hidden by the word "away."

---

**3. Quantify the Environmental Load**

The diagram shows the *shape* of the process. Now quantify the **load** — how much throughput the process is sending into the environment.

Have the student fill in:

```
ENVIRONMENTAL LOAD ESTIMATE

Material throughput (per week / month / year):
  Inputs consumed: _____ kg or units
  Waste generated: _____ kg or units

Energy use (rough estimate, optional):
  _____ kWh or "minimal" / "moderate" / "high"

Waste destination(s):
  Primary: _____ (landfill / recycling / atmosphere / waterway / etc.)
  Persistence: _____ (decomposes in X months / years / centuries / never)

Load on natural systems:
  Which absorbing system takes this load: _____
  Is that system saturated, near capacity, or fine: _____
```

Estimates are fine. Real engineering proposals start with estimates. The student should write down their assumptions: "I assumed each person produces 1 kg of food waste per week, and our household has 4 people, so 4 kg/week total."

---

**4. Where Does the Loop Open?**

Finally, the student identifies the exact point where the loop opens:

> "Look at your diagram. Trace each material from input to final destination. At what point does any material leave the system without a return path?"

That point is the **open loop**. The redesign will close it.

For the food-scrap example: the loop opens at the trash bag. After that, organic matter that *could* return to soil is instead routed to landfill, where it decomposes anaerobically and produces methane.

## Guided Session 2
### The Proposal Draft
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- write a structured engineering proposal with four standard sections
- frame the problem in systems terms (not emotional terms)
- describe the proposed circular redesign in concrete, specific language

---

#### Activities

**1. The Proposal Structure**

A formal engineering proposal has four sections. The student writes one at a time.

```
PROPOSAL: [Title — short, descriptive]

1. PROBLEM STATEMENT
   - Current state of the system
   - Inputs, throughput, waste stream
   - Environmental load
   - Where the loop is open

2. PROPOSED SOLUTION
   - The circular redesign
   - How the closed loop would work
   - Where the previously-waste output gets routed

3. REQUIRED RESOURCES
   - Materials needed (one-time and ongoing)
   - Time needed (setup and ongoing)
   - Space needed
   - Cooperation needed (who else has to participate)
   - Cost estimate

4. EXPECTED OUTCOMES
   - What changes in the system
   - Quantified reduction in waste output (estimate)
   - Secondary benefits or new outputs
   - What stays the same
```

---

**2. Write the Problem Statement**

The student writes the Problem Statement section. Voice rules:

- Specific numbers, not vague language.
- Engineering framing, not moral framing.
- Describe the system, not the people.

**Don't write:**
> "We waste so much food and it's terrible for the planet because methane from landfills is destroying the atmosphere and we have to fix this before it's too late."

**Do write:**
> "Our four-person household generates approximately 4 kg of food scraps per week. These scraps are placed in standard household trash, collected weekly by municipal pickup, and deposited in a regional landfill. In the landfill, the food scraps decompose anaerobically and release methane, a greenhouse gas roughly 80 times more potent than CO₂ over a 20-year timeframe. The current configuration has no return path for the organic matter — the biological nutrient cycle is open at the point of disposal."

The second version is shorter, more specific, and more useful. It also doesn't tell the reader how to feel.

---

**3. Write the Proposed Solution**

The student writes the Proposed Solution section. The structure mirrors the diagnosis:

```
PROPOSED CIRCULAR REDESIGN

[INPUTS] → [TRANSFORMATION] → [INTENDED OUTPUT]
                            ↓
                     [WASTE OUTPUT — now routed to:]
                            ↓
                     [RECOVERY PROCESS]
                            ↓
                     [RETURN PATH TO SYSTEM]
```

For the food-scrap example:

> "Food scraps from the household will be collected in a 5-liter kitchen compost pail. Twice per week, the pail will be emptied into a backyard compost bin. Over 3–6 months, the scraps will decompose into finished compost. The compost will be applied to the household vegetable garden in spring and fall. The proposed configuration returns the organic matter to a biological nutrient cycle on a closed-loop trajectory."

Notice the voice: specific, descriptive, neutral.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Finalize the first draft of the proposal. Sections 3 and 4 — Required Resources and Expected Outcomes — get drafted here.

### Activities

**1. Write Required Resources**

The student writes the Required Resources section honestly. The temptation here is to under-estimate what the redesign actually costs. Resist it.

```
REQUIRED RESOURCES

One-time materials:
  - _____ (e.g., compost bin: ~$60)
  - _____ (e.g., kitchen compost pail: ~$20)

Ongoing materials:
  - _____ (e.g., brown matter for compost balance — leaves from yard)

Time:
  - Setup: ~_____ hours
  - Ongoing: ~_____ minutes per week

Space:
  - _____ (e.g., ~2 sq ft in backyard, small countertop spot for pail)

Cooperation:
  - _____ (e.g., all family members must separate food scraps; one person must maintain the bin)

Estimated total cost: $_____ initial, $_____ ongoing per year
```

The proposal is more credible when the resource section is honest. A proposal that claims to cost nothing is not believable.

---

**2. Write Expected Outcomes**

The student writes the Expected Outcomes section. The format:

```
EXPECTED OUTCOMES

Waste reduction:
  Current waste stream: _____ kg/year to landfill
  After redesign: _____ kg/year (estimated reduction: _____%)

Secondary outputs:
  - _____ (e.g., ~50 kg of finished compost per year for garden use)

Changes in the system:
  - _____ (e.g., closed biological nutrient loop for household food scraps)

What stays the same:
  - _____ (e.g., grocery shopping, meal preparation, eating habits)
```

Numbers can be estimates. The student writes down their assumptions.

---

**3. Read the Whole Proposal Out Loud**

Once all four sections are drafted, the student reads the whole proposal out loud, start to finish.

Reading aloud catches:
- Sections that don't connect
- Claims that sound vague when spoken
- Places where the voice slips into moral framing
- Estimates that don't make sense when said in plain English

If the student finds something off, they revise. The first draft does not need to be polished — but it needs to *make sense as a whole*.

---

**4. Terrarium Check**

Week 12 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants — any changes?
- The terrarium is the existence proof: closed loops work. Your proposal is trying to do, at small scale, what the terrarium does effortlessly.

### Systems Log

> **Capstone topic:** ____
>
> **Diagram of current process:** [sketch attached]
>
> **Environmental load estimate:** ____
>
> **Where the loop opens:** ____
>
> **Problem statement (1 paragraph):** ____
>
> **Proposed solution (1 paragraph):** ____
>
> **Required resources:** ____
>
> **Expected outcomes:** ____
>
> **Terrarium update:** ____

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "Right now, ___ goes to ___."
- "Instead, ___ could go to ___."
- "To make this happen, we'd need ___."

### Reflection Questions

- What was the hardest part of writing the Problem Statement without using emotional language?
- Did you discover anything about your chosen process that you didn't know before drawing the diagram?
- Looking at your Required Resources list — what's the biggest cost? Is it money, time, space, or cooperation?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Draw the system:** "Show me the box-and-arrow diagram of your process." (Looking for: a complete diagram with labeled inputs, transformation, outputs, waste, and waste destination.)
2. **Quantify the load:** "How much waste does your process produce per week or per year?" (Looking for: a specific number, even if estimated, with stated assumptions.)
3. **Frame in systems language:** "Read me your problem statement." (Looking for: specific, neutral, descriptive — not emotional or moralizing.)

If the learner can do all 3, the proposal is ready for Week 16's specification work.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Notice how the proposal sounds. It doesn't try to convince anyone to feel guilty. It doesn't say 'we have to save the planet.' It just describes the system, states the problem, and proposes a redesign with honest tradeoffs."

This is what real engineering documents sound like. They are credible because they are honest, specific, and neutral. The work of *changing* the system is separate from the work of *describing* the system clearly. This week, the student is doing the describing.

> "If the description is accurate, the case for the redesign mostly makes itself. You don't need to convince anyone — the numbers do the convincing."

**This week's takeaway:** A good engineering proposal doesn't ask the reader to feel anything. It asks them to look at the system as it is, and consider a different configuration.
:::

:::tip Simplify (Ages 8–9)
Three sentences are enough for the proposal: "Right now, ___ ends up in ___." "Instead, ___ could go to ___." "To make this work, we'd need ___ and it would take ___ time." Draw two pictures (before and after) to go with the sentences.
:::

:::tip Extend (Ages 10–12)
After drafting the proposal, find one real-world engineering or sustainability report online (city composting program reports, corporate sustainability reports, EPA documents). Notice the voice. Notice the structure. How does it compare to what you wrote? Steal anything useful for your own proposal.
