# Week 14: Identifying and Fixing One Linear Process
*Unit 4: Re-Engineering the Interface*

This week is the practice round for the capstone. Students pick one specific open-loop process from their own life and sketch what a circular redesign would look like.

The capstone (Weeks 15–18) takes one such redesign all the way through diagnosis, engineering specification, stress-testing, and presentation. This week is a smaller version: pick, sketch, get feedback.

The point is to practice the diagnostic muscles built over the last three weeks (linear/circular API, biological/technical nutrients, designed obsolescence) before they have to do it for real.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- This is a low-stakes practice run for the capstone — the real proposal happens in Weeks 15–18.
- Students should pick a *small, specific* process. Not "fix climate change." Something like "what happens to the food scraps from one week of family meals."
- The redesign sketch should be honest about tradeoffs. A circular redesign isn't free — it costs space, time, money, or coordination.
- Use the work students do this week as the seed for the capstone project. Many students will continue with the same process they picked this week.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "Pick one thing in your house that gets thrown away. Think about how we could keep it from getting thrown away — by reusing it, by giving it to someone else, by composting it, or by buying it differently."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip the full engineering vocabulary. Use "what could we do instead."
- Skip the formal redesign sketch. A drawing of "before" and "after" is enough.

**Adapting the activities:**
- Use a single, very concrete process: "what happens to banana peels in our house?"
- Walk through the redesign collaboratively.

**Journal alternatives:** Two drawings: "How it works now" and "How we could change it." A single sentence each.

**What success looks like:** The learner can identify one thing that goes to the trash and one alternative way to handle it.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Push for specificity. Don't accept "we should recycle more" as a redesign. Accept "I will set up a compost bin in the back yard for fruit and vegetable scraps; here's the bin design; here are the rules for what goes in; here's how we'll empty it."
- Encourage estimation. How many pounds of food waste per year? How much compost would that produce?
- Have the student identify at least one real-world example of someone already doing the circular version of their process.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### Pick a Process
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- identify a specific open-loop process in their daily life
- describe its inputs, transformation, outputs, and waste stream
- name the environmental load it places on natural systems

---

#### Activities

**1. Brainstorm Candidates**

Have the student list 10 specific open-loop processes in their environment. Push for small and specific:

- Food scraps from family meals → trash → landfill
- School paper handouts → trash or recycling bin
- Delivery boxes from online shopping → trash or recycling
- Single-use water bottles at school → trash or recycling
- Outgrown clothes → donation or trash
- Old electronics in a drawer → eventually trash
- Holiday packaging → trash
- Used tissues → trash
- Coffee grounds (if relevant) → trash
- Dryer lint → trash
- Hair from haircuts → trash
- Pencil shavings → trash

The point is to see how many small open loops there are in everyday life.

---

**2. Pick One**

Out of the 10, pick **one** to focus on. Selection criteria:

- **Specific** — a single, well-defined process.
- **Personal** — something the student can actually observe or measure.
- **Tractable** — could plausibly be redesigned with available materials and effort.
- **Interesting** — something the student is curious about.

Don't worry if it seems small. The smaller the process, the easier it is to think clearly about it.

---

**3. Diagnose It**

For the chosen process, write a formal **systems diagnosis**:

```
PROCESS: ___________________

INPUTS:
- Material inputs: _____
- Energy inputs: _____
- Labor / attention required: _____

TRANSFORMATION:
- What happens to the inputs: _____

OUTPUTS (intended):
- The thing being produced: _____

OUTPUTS (unintended waste):
- What gets discarded: _____
- Where it actually goes: _____ (landfill, ocean, atmosphere, etc.)
- How long it persists: _____

LOOP STATUS:
- Where the loop is open: _____
- What absorbing system is taking the load: _____
```

This diagnostic is the foundation of any redesign. If you can't write a clean diagnosis, you don't yet understand the process well enough.

Example diagnosis (for the practice run):

```
PROCESS: Banana peels in a household of four

INPUTS:
- 1 banana per person per day (4 bananas) → 4 peels
- Energy: roughly zero (manual peeling)
- Labor: ~5 seconds per peel to throw away

TRANSFORMATION:
- Bananas eaten; peels are byproduct

OUTPUTS (intended):
- Food (the banana itself)

OUTPUTS (unintended waste):
- 4 peels per day = ~28 peels per week = ~1,460 peels per year
- Currently goes into trash bag → curbside trash → landfill
- In a landfill, peels decompose slowly under anaerobic conditions, producing methane (a greenhouse gas)
- A banana peel takes ~2 years to fully decompose in landfill conditions

LOOP STATUS:
- Open at end of peel's life — no return path to soil
- Absorbing system: municipal landfill
```

## Guided Session 2
### Sketch the Redesign
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- sketch a closed-loop alternative to the chosen open-loop process
- identify the resources and changes needed to operate the redesign
- name at least one tradeoff the redesign introduces

---

#### Activities

**1. The Redesign Sketch**

Now design the closed-loop alternative. For the banana peel example:

```
REDESIGNED PROCESS: Banana peels → home compost bin → garden soil

INPUTS:
- Same banana consumption
- A compost bin (one-time purchase, $30–80 or DIY)
- A small backyard space (~2 sq ft)
- ~30 seconds per week to carry compost bucket outside
- Possible "brown matter" (leaves, paper) for compost balance

TRANSFORMATION:
- Peels go into a kitchen compost bucket
- Once full, bucket is emptied into the backyard compost bin
- Over ~3-6 months, peels decompose into compost
- Compost is added to garden soil

OUTPUTS (intended):
- Food (the banana itself) — unchanged
- Garden compost (~5 lb per year of finished compost from peels alone)

OUTPUTS (unintended waste):
- Essentially zero — peels return to soil

LOOP STATUS:
- Closed — peels return to biological nutrient cycle

TRADEOFFS:
- Initial cost of compost bin (~$30–80)
- Small ongoing time cost (~5 min/week)
- Requires a backyard or a willing community compost program
- Need to manage compost balance (carbon-to-nitrogen ratio)
- Small smell/pest risk if not maintained
```

Notice the structure:

- **Before:** linear, no return path, peels go to landfill.
- **After:** closed loop, peels become soil amendment.
- **Costs:** real, but small.
- **Tradeoffs:** named honestly.

---

**2. The "Minimum Viable Loop"**

If the full redesign feels too ambitious, sketch a **minimum viable loop** — the simplest version that closes the loop, even partially.

Examples:

- Instead of a full backyard compost bin, just freeze food scraps and drop them at a community garden's compost pile once a week.
- Instead of a full electronics recycling system, just designate a bin and take items twice a year to a municipal e-waste day.
- Instead of zero packaging, just save and reuse delivery boxes for one round before recycling them.

The minimum viable loop is the engineering equivalent of "what's the smallest version that still works?"

---

**3. Find a Real-World Example**

For the chosen process, find one example of someone who has already done the redesign. This grounds the proposal in reality.

For composting:
- Look up community composting programs in your city.
- Look at apps like ShareWaste that connect food-waste producers with composters.
- Find a friend or neighbor who already composts.

For paper:
- Look at schools with paperless or low-paper systems.
- Look at offices that use double-sided printing as a default.

For clothing:
- Look at clothing swaps, thrift stores, repair cafés.

Existence of a real-world example proves the redesign is achievable, even if it requires effort.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Refine the redesign sketch and prepare for the capstone (Weeks 15–18).

### Activities

**1. The Full Sketch**

In the Systems Log, write up the complete redesign for your chosen process:

- The diagnosis (before)
- The redesigned process (after)
- The inputs needed for the redesign
- The tradeoffs honestly listed
- One real-world example of someone doing it

---

**2. Show It to Someone**

Show your redesign to one other person — a family member, friend, teacher. Ask them:

- Does it make sense?
- What questions do they have?
- What concerns would they have if you actually tried to implement it?

Write down their feedback. (This is the stress-test foreshadowing — Week 17 will go deeper.)

---

**3. Pick Your Capstone Topic**

This is the moment of commitment. For the rest of the curriculum (Weeks 15–18), you will be developing **one redesign** all the way through to a real proposal and presentation.

Your capstone topic can be:
- The same process you sketched this week (most common path).
- A bigger version of this week's topic (e.g., from "banana peels in my house" to "food waste at my school").
- A completely different process if this week's sketch revealed your interest is elsewhere.

Write down your capstone topic at the end of this week's Systems Log entry.

---

**4. Terrarium Check**

Week 11 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants — any changes?
- The terrarium has been operating as a fully circular system for almost 3 months. That's the existence proof you needed: closed loops can work.

### Systems Log

> **Today's System:** [Your chosen process]
>
> **Diagnosis (before):** [Full sketch]
>
> **Redesigned process (after):** [Full sketch]
>
> **Tradeoffs:** ____
>
> **Real-world example:** ____
>
> **Feedback from one person:** ____
>
> **My capstone topic:** ____
>
> **Terrarium update:** ____

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "Right now, ___ ends up in ___."
- "Instead, we could ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Pick something that wasn't on your candidate list. Could it be redesigned too? Or is it stuck as a linear process for some reason?
- The redesign you sketched has tradeoffs. Who benefits, and who pays for those tradeoffs? Are they the same person?
- If everyone in your town did your redesign tomorrow, what would change? What would the second-order effects be?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Diagnose a process:** "Pick a process in your house. What are its inputs and outputs?" (Looking for: an organized, specific answer.)
2. **Sketch a redesign:** "How would you close the loop?" (Looking for: a specific, plausible alternative — not just "recycle more.")
3. **Name a tradeoff:** "What does the redesigned process cost (in time, money, space, or coordination)?" (Looking for: honest acknowledgment of cost, not utopian framing.)

If the learner can do at least 2 of these, they're ready for the capstone (Week 15).

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Now that you've sketched one redesign, you have the template. Every open-loop process can be analyzed the same way. The framework — diagnose, redesign, identify tradeoffs — applies to anything."

This is the practice round. The capstone is for real. But the engineering mindset you used this week — see the process, name the open loop, sketch the closed loop — is now yours.

> "It doesn't matter how small your redesign is. What matters is that you can do the analysis. Once you can, you'll see open loops everywhere — and you'll know what it would take to close them."

**This week's takeaway:** Redesigning a process isn't magic. It's a structured engineering exercise: diagnose, propose, identify tradeoffs, validate against a real-world example.
:::
