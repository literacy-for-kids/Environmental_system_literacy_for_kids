# Optional Week 1: Amplifying Loops and Tipping Points
*Optional Extension — Advanced Systems Dynamics*

This is an optional extension week for learners who want to go deeper into one of the most important concepts in systems thinking: the difference between **balancing** and **amplifying** feedback loops, and the **tipping points** where a system flips from being one to the other.

Earlier in the curriculum, feedback loops were introduced casually — as the cause-and-effect cycles that make a system self-regulate. This week formalizes the distinction. Balancing loops self-correct. Amplifying loops self-reinforce. The two have completely different consequences for system stability.

The climate system runs on both. Some of its loops keep the planet stable. Some of its loops, once tipped past a threshold, take over and drive the system toward a new state regardless of what humans do afterward. The dangerous parts of climate change aren't the slow, linear warming — they're the amplifying loops that could lock in changes that can't be undone.

Engineering framing throughout. The student is studying system dynamics, not learning to fear climate change. The point is to recognize the pattern.

## Guided Session 1
### Balancing vs. Amplifying Loops
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- distinguish balancing feedback loops from amplifying feedback loops
- identify examples of each in everyday life and in natural systems
- explain why amplifying loops can drive systems to extreme states

---

#### Activities

**1. Recap: What's a Feedback Loop?**

Quick refresher. A feedback loop is when the output of a system affects its own input. The output loops back.

Examples:
- A household thermostat: the room gets too hot → the thermostat triggers the AC → the room cools down → the thermostat shuts off the AC.
- A microphone near a speaker: sound comes out of the speaker → the mic picks it up → the speaker amplifies it → the mic picks up the louder sound → speaker amplifies again → painful squeal.

Both are feedback loops. But they do opposite things.

---

**2. Balancing Loops**

A **balancing loop** (sometimes called "negative feedback" — but the word is misleading) brings the system *back toward* a target. It's self-correcting.

Examples:

- **Thermostat:** The room temperature is supposed to stay around 70°F. If it drifts up, the AC kicks in. If it drifts down, the heater kicks in. The system stabilizes around the target.
- **Body temperature:** Your body keeps itself around 37°C. If you get too hot, you sweat. If you get too cold, you shiver. The system stabilizes.
- **Predator-prey:** When prey populations get high, predators eat well and multiply. When predators get numerous, prey populations drop. When prey populations are low, predators starve and decline. Both populations oscillate around a stable middle.
- **Hunger:** Empty stomach → hungry → eat → full → not hungry. The signal turns off when the goal is met.

Balancing loops produce **stability**. The system finds an equilibrium and stays near it, even when disturbed.

Draw a balancing loop:

```
         (correction)
              ↑
              |
   STATE → DETECTOR
              |
              ↓
     (action that reduces deviation from target)
```

---

**3. Amplifying Loops**

An **amplifying loop** (sometimes called "positive feedback" — also misleading) makes the system *move further* from where it started. It's self-reinforcing.

Examples:

- **Microphone squeal:** Sound out → louder sound out → even louder → ear-splitting noise. No stable point.
- **Compound interest:** Money in savings → earns interest → larger balance → earns more interest → even larger balance. Growth amplifies itself.
- **Viral spread (social media or biological):** One person shares / infects another → that person shares with two more → exponential growth, until something else limits it.
- **A snowball rolling downhill:** Snow sticks to the ball → bigger ball → more snow sticks → bigger ball → bigger.
- **A panic or a bank run:** People worry the bank will fail → withdraw money → bank gets weaker → more people worry → more withdraw → bank actually fails.

Amplifying loops produce **growth, collapse, or runaway behavior** — depending on what they're amplifying. They don't produce stability. Either something else stops them (a limit), or the system goes to an extreme.

Draw an amplifying loop:

```
   STATE → DRIVER
      ↑       |
      |       ↓
      +-(action that increases deviation from start)
```

---

**4. Quick Sort: Balancing or Amplifying?**

The student looks at each example and labels it.

| Example | Balancing or Amplifying? |
|---|---|
| Population of foxes and rabbits in a forest | Balancing (predator-prey) |
| A virus spreading through a school | Amplifying (until immunity or isolation breaks it) |
| Your body's temperature regulation | Balancing |
| Fire feeding on a forest (heat dries fuel → more fire → more heat) | Amplifying |
| A crowded subway train: more people → less personal space → some get off | Balancing |
| Erosion: water carves channel → channel funnels more water → channel deepens | Amplifying |
| A toddler's tantrum: cries → parent reacts → cries louder → parent more upset → louder cries | Amplifying (until someone breaks the loop) |
| A sleeping bag warming you up to a comfortable temperature | Balancing |

The student should be able to label each one and explain why.

---

**5. Why the Distinction Matters**

Balancing loops are *forgiving*. If you push the system, it pushes back. You can experiment with disturbances because the system self-corrects.

Amplifying loops are *unforgiving*. If you push the system, it doesn't just stay pushed — it accelerates in the direction you pushed. By the time you notice, the system might be much further from where it started than you intended.

Most engineered systems try to include strong balancing loops (thermostats, regulators, safety mechanisms) and avoid amplifying loops (or contain them tightly). Most natural systems have a mix of both. The question is which loops dominate under which conditions.

## Guided Session 2
### Climate Amplifying Loops and Tipping Points
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- describe three amplifying feedback loops in the climate system
- explain what a tipping point is and why it changes the rules
- distinguish reversible from potentially irreversible system changes

---

#### Activities

**1. The Three Big Climate Amplifying Loops**

The climate system has many feedback loops. Three of the most significant amplifying ones are worth understanding in detail.

---

**Loop 1: Ice-Albedo Feedback**

**Albedo** is how reflective a surface is. Polar sea ice and continental ice sheets reflect about 80–90% of incoming sunlight back to space. The dark ocean surface and bare land reflect only about 10–20% — meaning they absorb 80–90% of the incoming sunlight as heat.

The loop:

```
   Warming → ice melts → exposed darker ocean/land → absorbs more sunlight
                                                              ↓
                                                       more warming
                                                              ↓
                                                       (loop continues)
```

The Arctic has warmed roughly four times faster than the global average since 1980, partly because of this feedback. As sea ice retreats, the newly-exposed Arctic Ocean absorbs more solar energy, which speeds up further melting.

This is an amplifying loop. The melting drives more warming, which drives more melting.

---

**Loop 2: Permafrost Carbon Feedback**

**Permafrost** is ground that has remained frozen for at least two consecutive years. The Arctic permafrost contains an estimated **~1,500 billion tonnes of carbon** in frozen organic matter — roughly twice the amount of carbon currently in the atmosphere. (Source: IPCC AR6 and related literature; estimates vary, but this order of magnitude is widely accepted.)

That organic matter has been frozen for thousands of years, so the carbon hasn't entered the active cycle. As long as it stays frozen, it's effectively in storage.

The loop:

```
   Warming → permafrost thaws → organic matter decomposes
                                       ↓
                            CO₂ and methane released
                                       ↓
                                more warming
                                       ↓
                               (loop continues)
```

This is an amplifying loop on a long timescale. The permafrost carbon pool is large enough that significant release could add meaningfully to atmospheric carbon, accelerating warming further.

---

**Loop 3: Water Vapor Feedback**

Warmer air can hold more water vapor (roughly 7% more per °C of warming, by basic physics — the Clausius-Clapeyron relation). Water vapor itself is a greenhouse gas — actually the most abundant one in the atmosphere.

The loop:

```
   Warming → air holds more water vapor → water vapor traps heat
                                                   ↓
                                              more warming
                                                   ↓
                                            (loop continues)
```

This is the **largest amplifying feedback in the climate system** in terms of warming effect — but it's a **fast feedback**. The atmosphere reaches its new water-vapor equilibrium within days or weeks. So this loop doesn't keep amplifying indefinitely. It roughly *doubles* the warming effect of any other driver (like CO₂), and then settles into a new equilibrium.

The first two loops (ice-albedo and permafrost) are **slow feedbacks** — they operate over years to centuries. They're more dangerous in the sense that they can lock in over long timescales.

---

**2. Tipping Points**

A **tipping point** is a threshold beyond which a system's amplifying loops take over and the system can no longer self-correct.

Below the tipping point: the system can be pushed in one direction, but balancing loops bring it back.

Above the tipping point: the amplifying loops dominate. The system continues to move in the same direction even if the original disturbance stops.

Analogy: a chair sitting on a sloped floor. Push it gently to one side, and it stays close to where it started. Push it past the point of balance, and it tips over — and no amount of pushing back can right it without externally lifting it.

---

**3. Candidate Climate Tipping Points**

Some climate subsystems are considered potential tipping points. Each one is an active area of research, and the exact thresholds are uncertain.

**West Antarctic Ice Sheet:** Once collapse begins, the geometry of the ice sheet (it sits below sea level in many places) may make the collapse self-sustaining. Even if warming stopped, the ice loss might continue for centuries.

**Amazon Rainforest:** Deforestation plus drought may shift the Amazon from a self-watering rainforest to a savanna. The rainforest generates its own rainfall through evapotranspiration; if enough of it is removed, the system can't sustain itself.

**Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC):** The ocean current system that includes the Gulf Stream depends on dense, salty water sinking in the North Atlantic. Freshwater from melting Greenland ice could weaken this sinking and slow or stop the circulation. If it stops, restarting it may not be possible in any near-term timeframe.

**Permafrost:** Sufficient warming could trigger broad-scale thaw that releases enough carbon to lock in further warming, even if other emissions stopped.

In each case, the system isn't just changing slowly — it's potentially crossing a threshold beyond which the change becomes self-sustaining.

---

**4. Hysteresis**

**Hysteresis** is the property that once a system has tipped, it doesn't necessarily snap back when conditions return to the original level.

Imagine a switch that's hard to flip in either direction. Once you flip it on, it stays on even if you remove the force. To flip it back off, you have to push much harder than you'd expect.

The climate system has hysteresis at multiple scales. Once an ice sheet has lost a critical fraction of its mass, just reducing emissions back to original levels doesn't regrow the ice on any human timescale. The system has moved to a new attractor state.

This is why tipping points matter so much: they introduce **irreversibility** on relevant timescales. A slow, reversible change is one thing. A change that locks in for centuries is another.

---

**5. Early-Warning Signals (Brief, Advanced)**

Complex-systems researchers have identified statistical signals that sometimes appear before a system reaches a tipping point. At the appropriate level of detail for this curriculum:

- **Increasing variance:** The system's behavior gets more erratic. Bigger swings up and down.
- **Slower recovery from disturbances:** When the system is pushed, it takes longer to return to its baseline.
- **"Flickering" between two states:** The system briefly visits the new state, then returns — and then visits again, more often.

These aren't predictions of *when* a tip will happen. They're hints that the system may be losing the resilience it once had. Real-world examples are debated, and detecting these signals reliably is hard.

The student doesn't need to memorize the signals. They just need to know that this is an active area of research, and that complex systems sometimes warn before they tip — if anyone is watching the right indicators.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Map three feedback loops in the student's own life and identify one to convert.

### Activities

**1. The Three Loops Exercise**

The student maps three feedback loops in their personal life:

```
LOOP 1 — BALANCING

What's the loop:
Why it's balancing:
What target does it stabilize around:

LOOP 2 — AMPLIFYING

What's the loop:
Why it's amplifying:
What stops it from running away (if anything):

LOOP 3 — AMBIGUOUS (could be either)

What's the loop:
What determines which kind it is:
```

Examples:

**Balancing loops in everyday life:**
- Getting hungry → eating → satiated → not hungry
- Getting tired → going to sleep → resting → waking refreshed
- Room gets messy → frustration builds → cleaning happens → room is clean
- Saving money: spending too fast → bank account drops → notice → spend less

**Amplifying loops in everyday life:**
- Practice a skill → get better → enjoy it more → practice more → get even better
- Skip homework one day → less prep → harder to follow class → less interest → skip more
- Social media: scroll a little → algorithm shows similar content → engaging → scroll more → algorithm doubles down
- Frustration cycle: small annoyance → react angrily → others react → more annoyance → bigger anger

**Ambiguous loops:**
- Friendship: spending time together. Could be balancing (you reach a comfortable amount of contact and stay there) or amplifying (the closer you get, the more time you spend, the closer you get).
- Confidence: doing well at something. Could feed on itself (amplifying) or hit a ceiling where you stop improving (balancing).

---

**2. Convert an Amplifying Loop**

Pick the amplifying loop the student identified. If it's headed in a bad direction, what would convert it into a balancing one?

The general strategies:

- **Add a limiter:** Cap the amount of the amplifying behavior (screen-time limits, savings minimums, etc.)
- **Add a competing balancing loop:** Counter-pressure that gets stronger as the amplifying behavior gets stronger (a friend who pulls you back when you spiral)
- **Change the input:** Remove what's being amplified at the source (remove the social media app from the home screen)
- **Reset the loop periodically:** Force the system back to baseline at intervals (weekly review, monthly reset, etc.)

The student picks one conversion strategy and writes it down.

---

**3. Look at the Capstone Through This Lens**

If the student wants to: re-examine their capstone proposal from earlier in the curriculum. Does it create any amplifying loops, intentional or accidental? Does it weaken any balancing loops? Does it have a tipping point of its own (a minimum participation level below which it collapses)?

This is optional reflection — but it can sharpen the capstone proposal.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Most of the dangerous parts of climate change aren't the slow, linear warming. They're the amplifying loops — places where a small change triggers a bigger one, which triggers a bigger one, and so on. The system can become its own driver."

This is why "just stop emitting carbon" isn't a complete description of the problem. If amplifying loops have been triggered, the system continues to move even without further input. The system has its own momentum now.

> "It's also why tipping points are so important — and why so much climate research focuses on them. The question isn't just 'how much will it warm?' It's 'are we approaching any thresholds where the amplifying loops take over?'"

**This week's takeaway:** Amplifying loops produce nonlinear behavior. Systems with tipping points don't change smoothly — they jump. Understanding this is the difference between thinking the planet is a thermostat (linear) and thinking it's a complex system with potential phase changes (nonlinear).
:::

Apply this to a real system you care about.
