# Capstone Rubric

Use this rubric across Weeks 15-18. Score only the evidence the student actually produced. A younger student may show strong thinking with a simpler artifact. An older student should usually show more detail, measurement, and explicit tradeoff reasoning.

### Suggested scale

- 1 = Beginning
- 2 = Developing
- 3 = Secure
- 4 = Strong

---

## Rubric Table

| Criterion | 1 - Beginning | 2 - Developing | 3 - Secure | 4 - Strong |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| System diagnosis | Names a problem but not the system | Identifies some inputs, outputs, or waste but misses key parts | Clearly diagnoses the current process and where the loop is open | Diagnoses the process clearly and identifies bottlenecks, missing return paths, and scope boundaries |
| Box-and-arrow diagram | Diagram is incomplete or confusing | Diagram shows the main parts but some arrows or labels are unclear | Diagram clearly shows inputs, transformation, outputs, and waste path | Diagram is clear, accurate, and shows important relationships, decision points, or rates |
| Quantitative load estimate | No estimate, or estimate is not usable | Gives a rough estimate with limited support | Gives a reasonable estimate with units or timeframe | Gives a reasonable estimate, explains method, and names uncertainty honestly |
| Proposed circular redesign | Solution is vague or unrelated to diagnosis | Solution matches the diagnosis but is underspecified | Redesign directly closes part of the loop in a plausible way | Redesign is plausible, scoped well, and shows a thoughtful return path for outputs |
| Specifications | Few or no measurable details | Includes some operational details | Includes clear specs for time, space, materials, and expected output | Includes clear specs plus thresholds, monitoring points, or success conditions |
| Constraints and tradeoffs | Ignores practical limits | Names one or two constraints | Names important constraints and at least one real tradeoff | Weighs tradeoffs honestly and shows design choices made because of them |
| Failure modes | Says the design might fail but not how | Lists failure modes without recovery steps | Identifies likely failure modes and gives recovery or prevention ideas | Identifies likely failure modes, edge cases, and how the design tolerates partial failure |
| Stakeholder analysis | Stakeholders are missing or reduced to villains | Names affected people but reasons are thin | Names key stakeholders and gives reasonable concerns | Steel-mans objections and adjusts the design or pitch in response |
| Pitch clarity | Audience cannot easily follow the proposal | Main idea is understandable but structure wanders | Pitch is organized, concise, and understandable | Pitch is tightly structured and easy to follow from current state to redesign |
| Neutral engineering voice | Relies on slogans, blame, or guilt language | Mostly neutral but drifts into persuasion | Uses clear, neutral systems language | Uses disciplined, neutral language and lets evidence carry the argument |
| Response to questions | Answers are evasive or unsupported | Answers some questions but becomes vague or defensive | Answers questions clearly and admits uncertainty when needed | Answers questions clearly, revises in real time, and uses objections productively |
| Version 2.0 reflection | No meaningful revision note | Revision note is generic | Identifies what would change in the next version | Identifies specific revisions, new evidence needed, and remaining limitations |

## Interpreting Scores

### Ages 8-9

For younger learners, a score of 3 often looks like:

- a simple but correct diagram
- a concrete redesign that could work in one place
- one or two clear measurements or estimates
- spoken answers that show real understanding

Do not require adult-style polish.

### Ages 10-12

For older learners, a score of 3 should usually include:

- a labeled diagram
- measurable specs
- an explicit tradeoff
- at least three plausible failure modes
- a short but coherent response to questions

---

## Fast Scoring Option

If you do not want to score every row, prioritize these six:

1. System diagnosis
2. Box-and-arrow diagram
3. Quantitative load estimate
4. Proposed circular redesign
5. Constraints and tradeoffs
6. Response to questions

That gives a fast but still meaningful picture of whether the capstone actually works as systems analysis.

---

## Recommended Evidence Bundle

When possible, score the capstone using all of these together:

- Week 15 diagnosis page
- Week 16 Specs Sheet
- Week 17 failure-mode and stakeholder notes
- Week 18 pitch outline and post-pitch reflection
- relevant Systems Log entries

This rubric works best when it is used across the full capstone arc, not only on the final talk.
