# Week 18: Presenting a Real Solution to a Real Audience
*Unit 5: The Capstone — Final Presentation*

The capstone ends here.

Eighteen weeks ago, the curriculum opened with the planet as an engineering system — a closed material loop powered by an external energy source, with all the structural properties of a designed system. Over the next 14 weeks, the student learned how the system works (water cycle, carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle), how it absorbs load (carrying capacity, feedback loops), and where the industrial economy violates its design constraints (linear API, designed obsolescence, open loops).

Then over the last three weeks, they took one specific open loop from their own life — and engineered a real proposal to close it.

This week, they present that proposal to a real audience. Not a simulation. Not a classroom exercise. A real person who matters to the student — a family member, teacher, neighbor, community member — hears the proposal and responds.

The presentation is short: about five minutes. The structure is fixed. The voice is engineering throughout. After the pitch, the student reflects on what version 2.0 of the proposal would look like, based on what the audience said.

This is what the eighteen weeks were preparing for.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- The audience does not have to be large. One real person is enough. The point is that it's *real*.
- The presentation should be five minutes, not fifty. Tight, structured, neutral.
- Engineering framing all the way through. "This process generates 4 kg of food waste per week" — not "we have to do something about waste."
- After the pitch, expect questions and objections. That's success, not failure. Real audiences engage.
- Celebrate completion at the end of this week. The student has done something difficult and finished it.
:::

## Facilitator Preparation

:::info Before You Begin
- Help the student identify and confirm a real audience by mid-week. One person is enough. Family members are the easiest if no other audience is available.
- Set a specific time for the pitch. "Saturday at 3pm, in the kitchen" beats "sometime this week."
- Help with optional visuals: the box-and-arrow diagrams from Week 15 work well as visual aids. So does the Specs Sheet.
- After the pitch, give the student space for reflection — don't rush to evaluate the result.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
**The pitch is the work, not the performance.** The student isn't being graded on charisma. They're presenting an engineering proposal. Neutral voice, clear structure, honest tradeoffs.

**Audience questions are a gift.** If the audience asks something the student hadn't anticipated, that's information for version 2.0 — not a failure of the pitch.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "You're going to tell one person about your plan. You'll explain what's happening now, what you'd change, and why. They'll ask questions, and you'll answer them. That's it."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Cut the pitch to 2–3 minutes.
- Skip the structured 5-section format. Use a 3-sentence pitch: "Right now, ___ happens. We could change it by ___. To do that, we'd need ___."
- Skip formal Q&A. Just let the audience react naturally.

**Adapting the activities:**
- The audience can be a stuffed animal or pet for practice, then a family member for the real version.
- Drawings make excellent visual aids — encourage the learner to use the box-and-arrow diagram from Week 15.
- Celebrate completion big. The learner just did the same thing professional engineers do.

**Journal alternatives:** "I told ___ about my plan. They said ___. I felt ___."

**What success looks like:** The learner clearly explains their plan to a real person in their own words.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Full five-minute structured pitch with the 5 standard sections.
- Audience should be someone the proposal might actually involve (a parent if it's a household redesign, a teacher if it's a school redesign).
- Encourage a Q&A period after the pitch.
- Post-pitch, the student writes a full v2.0 reflection — what they'd change about both the proposal and the pitch itself.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### Preparing the Pitch
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- structure a five-minute presentation around five standard sections
- communicate the proposal in neutral, engineering language
- prepare for likely audience questions and objections

---

#### Activities

**1. The Five-Section Structure**

The pitch follows a fixed structure. Each section is roughly one minute.

```
1. CURRENT STATE (~60 seconds)
   - What's the open-loop process you're addressing
   - Concrete numbers: how much throughput, what waste stream
   - What the environmental load is

2. WHY IT MATTERS (~60 seconds)
   - Framed as a systems issue, not a moral one
   - "This process generates 4 kg of food waste per week, which currently
      goes to landfill, where it decomposes anaerobically and produces methane"
   - Not "we have to save the planet"

3. THE PROPOSED SOLUTION (~60 seconds)
   - The circular redesign in one or two sentences
   - The diagram, if you have it as a visual aid
   - What the closed loop looks like

4. WHAT IT REQUIRES (~60 seconds)
   - Honest about resources, time, money, space, behavior
   - Constraints and tradeoffs named explicitly
   - "We gain X but pay Y"
   - Known limitations

5. EXPECTED OUTCOMES (~60 seconds)
   - What changes in the system
   - The success metric with a target number
   - Secondary effects (positive and negative)
```

The student writes one or two sentences per section on an outline page. Total: ~10–12 sentences. That's the entire pitch.

---

**2. Writing the Pitch Outline**

The pitch is short. Every sentence has to earn its place. Have the student write the outline, then prune it.

Examples of how each section sounds in finished form:

**Current State:**
> "Our household generates approximately 4 kg of food scraps per week — about 200 kg per year. These scraps currently go into the regular trash and end up at the regional landfill. The trash bag is the point where the biological nutrient cycle opens."

**Why It Matters:**
> "In a landfill, food scraps decompose anaerobically — without oxygen — and produce methane. Methane is a greenhouse gas roughly 80 times more potent than CO₂ over a 20-year timeframe. The current configuration takes material that could return to soil and instead loads the atmosphere."

**Proposed Solution:**
> "I'm proposing a household composting system. Food scraps go from the kitchen pail into an outdoor bin, decompose over three to six months, and become compost for the garden. The biological nutrient loop closes."

**What It Requires:**
> "Initial cost: about $80 for the bin and pail. Ongoing time: about 5 minutes a week to empty the pail. Space: about 2 square feet in the backyard. The main behavioral change is sorting scraps at point-of-use. Known limitations: this design depends on at least one household member operating the bin, and the redesign doesn't address food waste at the upstream agricultural level."

**Expected Outcomes:**
> "We'd reduce household landfill-bound waste by about 50% by weight. We'd produce around 30 kg of compost per year for garden use. The biological nutrient cycle closes for our food scraps. The metric I'd track is kilograms of food waste sent to landfill per month, with a target of 2 kg or less."

Five sections. Each ~60 seconds. The whole pitch is ~5 minutes.

---

**3. The Voice Test**

Read the pitch out loud once. Then go back and check:

- Did you say "we have to" or "we need to" anywhere? Replace with "this design proposes" or "the redesign would."
- Did you say "terrible," "horrible," "destroy"? Replace with neutral descriptors and let the numbers carry the weight.
- Did you say "I think we should"? Replace with "this proposal recommends" or just delete the phrase.
- Did you use vague intensifiers ("really," "very," "incredibly")? Cut them.

The voice should be specific, neutral, and short. The pitch is a description of a system change. The audience can form their own emotional response to the numbers — that's not the presenter's job.

---

**4. Anticipating Questions**

The audience will ask questions. The student writes down likely ones and drafts short answers:

| Likely question | One-sentence answer |
|---|---|
| "How do you know it's really 4 kg per week?" | "Estimated by counting scraps over one week — about 1 kg per person." |
| "Won't the bin smell?" | "If the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio is balanced with brown matter, the smell stays minimal. We exclude meat and dairy to keep that simple." |
| "What if you forget to empty it?" | "The pail is sized to last about a week, and we have a weekly reminder. A skipped week is a minor problem, not a critical one." |
| "Why not just put it all in the trash like before?" | "Both work. The redesign closes the nutrient loop. Whether that's worth the cost is a judgment call." |
| "Have you actually started?" | (Honest answer.) |

The student is not memorizing answers — they're rehearsing the *kind* of answer. Specific, neutral, brief.

## Guided Session 2
### The Pitch
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- deliver the five-minute pitch to a real audience
- respond to audience questions and objections clearly
- distinguish constructive feedback from objection-as-rejection

---

#### Activities

**1. Set the Stage**

The pitch happens to a real person. Set up:

- A specific time and place
- The audience is told it'll be about 5 minutes plus a few questions
- The student has their notes, visual aids, and the Specs Sheet
- A timer if available (don't be rigid about it, but aim for ~5 minutes)

If multiple people are available, multiple audience members can attend. One is enough.

---

**2. Deliver the Pitch**

The student delivers all five sections. The facilitator stays in the audience role — not coaching mid-pitch.

If the student trips up, that's fine. The pitch doesn't need to be perfect. The goal is: did the audience understand what's being proposed, why, and what it costs?

---

**3. Take Questions**

After the pitch, open it for questions. The audience asks what they want. The student answers as best they can.

Three principles for handling questions:

- **If you don't know, say so.** "That's a good question. I don't have a number for that. My guess is around X, but I'd want to check."
- **If the question reveals a flaw, acknowledge it.** "You're right — I didn't think about that. That'd be a real failure mode."
- **If the question is a real objection, take it seriously.** Don't argue back. Note it. The objection might be valid.

Real audiences notice when the presenter is being honest versus defensive. Honest beats defensive every time.

---

**4. Receive the Verdict**

After Q&A, ask the audience:

- "What part made the most sense to you?"
- "What part are you skeptical about?"
- "Is this something you'd be willing to participate in / approve / support? Why or why not?"

The audience may say yes, no, or "maybe, with changes." All three are valid responses. The student is not failing if the answer is "no" or "maybe." Real engineering proposals don't all get accepted. The point is to have heard the response clearly enough to revise the proposal — or to decide whether to revise the audience instead.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Reflect on the pitch and draft version 2.0 of the proposal.

### Activities

**1. Post-Pitch Reflection**

In the Systems Log, write:

```
POST-PITCH REFLECTION

Date of pitch: _____
Audience: _____

What I said well: _____

What I'd say differently next time: _____

Questions I hadn't anticipated: _____

Objections I heard: _____

The objection I think was most reasonable: _____

The objection I think was least reasonable (and why): _____

What the audience said the strongest part of the proposal was: _____

What the audience said the weakest part of the proposal was: _____
```

---

**2. Version 2.0 Notes**

Based on the pitch, draft what the proposal would look like in version 2.0:

```
PROPOSAL v2.0 — WHAT WOULD CHANGE

To address [audience's main objection], I would: _____

To address [audience's second concern], I would: _____

A new known limitation I would add: _____

Something the audience pointed out that I want to think about more: _____

Whether I'd actually attempt to implement this proposal: _____ (yes / no / yes-with-modifications)
```

If the student decides to actually implement the redesign — fantastic. If not, that's also valid; some proposals are exercises in engineering thinking, not commitments to action. The point is that the student knows the difference.

---

**3. Terrarium Final Check**

Week 15 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants — any final observations?
- The terrarium has been operating as a closed loop for the entire capstone phase. It is the existence proof for everything the capstone proposed: closed loops can work, given the right architecture. Your engineered redesign has more constraints than the terrarium did — but the principle is the same.

### Reflection Questions

## Check for Understanding

This is the final check for the entire course. After the pitch and reflection, confirm:

1. **Concept retention:** "Name three big ideas from this course." (Looking for any three — water cycle, carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle, carrying capacity, feedback loops, tragedy of the commons, linear/circular API, biological/technical nutrients, designed obsolescence, etc.)
2. **Engineering framing:** "Describe an open-loop process in your house using engineering language." (Looking for: neutral, specific, structural — not moralizing.)
3. **Personal framework:** "If you came across a new environmental issue tomorrow, what's your process for thinking about it?" (Looking for: diagnose the system → find where the loop opens → propose a redesign → name the tradeoffs.)
4. **Pitch transferability:** "Could you give this kind of pitch about a different process?" (Looking for: yes, with the same structure.)

If the learner can do all four, they've completed the course successfully. The engineering mindset is theirs now.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
This is the final Pause and Notice for the course. Make it count.

> "Eighteen weeks ago, the planet was probably a 'place' to you — something you lived on, something with weather, something with nature. After this course, it's also a system. With cycles. With limits. With feedback loops. With places where the industrial economy has built linear processes on top of a circular substrate, and where the two are clashing."

> "You can now describe the water cycle, the carbon cycle, the nitrogen cycle, carrying capacity, feedback loops, and the linear-versus-circular API mismatch. You can trace a product from raw material to end-of-life. You can design a proposal to close an open loop and stress-test it before presenting it. That's a lot."

> "Most adults can't do this. Most adults haven't ever been given a system framework for thinking about the environment — only an emotional one. You now have both, and the structural one is the more useful one, because it tells you where to push to change something."

**Course takeaway:** The environment is an engineering system. Once you can see it that way, the question stops being "is this bad?" and becomes "what's the system, where is it failing, and what would it cost to redesign it?" That's the question worth carrying out of this course.
:::
