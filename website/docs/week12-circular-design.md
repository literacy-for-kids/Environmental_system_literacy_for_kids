# Week 12: How to Close an Open Loop
*Unit 4: Re-Engineering the Interface*

Last week we named the linear API and identified the mismatch it creates with natural systems. This week we look at how engineers close the loop.

The big idea: **waste is a design failure.** If an output has no use, that means the system was designed without a return path. A redesigned system would route every output back into another process as an input. This is called the **circular economy** or **cradle-to-cradle design**.

There are two main categories of materials in a well-designed circular system:

- **Biological nutrients** — materials that can safely return to biological cycles (compost, biodegrade, feed plants and microbes). Wood, cotton, food scraps, paper.
- **Technical nutrients** — materials that can cycle indefinitely through industrial use (recycled, remanufactured, refurbished). Aluminum, steel, glass, well-designed plastics.

The catch: most products on the market today **mix** biological and technical nutrients in ways that make both useless. A tetrapak (paper + plastic + foil laminated together) can't be composted *or* recycled cleanly. A child's toy with bonded plastic, metal, and electronics is even worse.

This week we look at how to redesign to keep the streams separate.

---

- Have the Systems Log handy.
- Optional: look up a brief description of **Kalundborg Symbiosis** in Denmark — one of the most famous real-world examples of industrial symbiosis.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
This is the most optimistic week of the curriculum. Don't oversell it.

Circular design works in some contexts (aluminum cans are a stunning success). It's emerging in others (modular electronics, refillable packaging). And it's hard or impossible in some (most chemicals, single-use medical equipment).

The frame: this is engineering, not utopianism. Some loops can be closed; others can't (yet). Knowing which is which is the skill.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "Everything we make should be designed so that when we're done with it, it can become something else — either compost for the soil, or material for a new product."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip industrial symbiosis. Use the simpler "compost vs. recycling" sort.
- Skip downcycling. Keep it simple: "biological things go back to dirt; technical things can be melted down again."

**Adapting the activities:**
- Sort the household items into two piles: "this can go back to nature" vs. "this can be remade into something else."

**Journal alternatives:** Two columns — "biological nutrients" with drawings of things that go back to soil, and "technical nutrients" with drawings of things that can be melted and reused.

**What success looks like:** The learner can sort objects into compostable vs. recyclable vs. trash, and explain why each goes where.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Discuss the difference between **upcycling** (turning waste into something more valuable), **recycling** (turning waste into similar-quality material), and **downcycling** (turning waste into lower-quality material).
- Look up actual aluminum recycling rates and the energy savings (~95% energy reduction vs. virgin aluminum production). Why is aluminum the gold standard of technical nutrient cycling?
- Discuss why plastic recycling has been less successful — the chemistry of polymer degradation, sorting challenges, contamination, and economic factors.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### Biological and Technical Nutrients
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- distinguish biological nutrients from technical nutrients
- identify mixed-material products that block both nutrient cycles
- describe cradle-to-cradle design as separating materials so they can stay in productive cycles

---

#### Activities

**1. The Two Streams**

Introduce the framework:

> "In a well-designed circular system, every material should belong to one of two streams. **Biological nutrients** safely return to soil through composting or natural decomposition. **Technical nutrients** can be melted down or otherwise reprocessed indefinitely into new products."

Examples of biological nutrients:
- Food waste, paper, cardboard, untreated wood, cotton, wool, leather (untreated), bamboo

Examples of technical nutrients:
- Aluminum, steel, copper, gold, glass, certain well-formulated plastics (PET, HDPE, when collected cleanly)

The crucial requirement: **the two streams cannot be mixed.** Once mixed, neither one cycles well.

> "If you laminate paper to plastic, you can't compost it (the plastic ruins the compost) and you can't recycle it (the paper ruins the plastic stream). It just becomes garbage."

---

**2. The Sort**

Lay out the household items you gathered. For each, have the student categorize:

- **Pure biological nutrient** (e.g., banana peel, untreated cardboard)
- **Pure technical nutrient** (e.g., aluminum can, glass jar)
- **Mixed/contaminated** (e.g., laminated juice carton, plastic-coated paper plate, multi-material toy)
- **Designed to cycle** (e.g., a glass bottle that gets returned and refilled)
- **Designed to be discarded** (e.g., most single-use plastics)

After sorting, discuss: which pile is biggest? In most homes, "mixed/contaminated" or "designed to be discarded" dominate.

---

**3. The Cradle-to-Cradle Concept**

Introduce the term:

> "Cradle-to-cradle is a design philosophy where every product is designed from the start so that when it's done being used, its materials become inputs to other products. Not 'cradle-to-grave' — there's no grave. Every grave becomes a cradle for something else."

Compare to the linear "cradle-to-grave" model that dominates manufacturing today:

| Cradle-to-Grave (Linear) | Cradle-to-Cradle (Circular) |
|---|---|
| Mine raw material | Reclaim previous-cycle material |
| Manufacture product | Manufacture product (designed for return) |
| Sell product | Sell product (sometimes leased instead of sold) |
| Customer uses it | Customer uses it |
| Customer discards it | Customer returns it / it's collected |
| Disposal: landfill or incinerator | Materials reenter the appropriate stream (biological or technical) |
| Loop is open | Loop is closed |

Examples of cradle-to-cradle products that exist today:

- **Aluminum cans:** ~75% of all aluminum ever produced is still in use because aluminum recycles indefinitely with high quality retention.
- **Refillable glass bottles** (still common in some countries for milk and beer): one bottle can cycle 30+ times.
- **Modular office carpet tiles** (companies like Interface): worn tiles are collected, the backing reground, and new tiles made with no loss of quality.
- **Some clothing brands** (Patagonia, Eileen Fisher, etc.) accept old garments and remanufacture them into new ones or fabric for other uses.

## Guided Session 2
### Industrial Symbiosis
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- describe industrial symbiosis as one factory's output becoming another factory's input
- explain why circular design often requires coordination across multiple businesses
- recognize that closing the loop usually requires planning ahead, not just sorting after the fact

---

#### Activities

**1. The Kalundborg Example**

Tell the story:

In Kalundborg, Denmark, a group of factories and the municipal services have, over decades, built up an intricate web of waste exchanges. Today it works roughly like this:

- A power plant produces electricity. Its waste heat (in the form of warm water) is piped to nearby homes for heating. Its steam is piped to a refinery and to a Novo Nordisk insulin plant.
- The refinery produces sulfur as a byproduct of cleaning sulfur out of oil. It sells the sulfur to a sulfuric acid plant.
- The power plant scrubs flue gas to remove sulfur dioxide. The byproduct, gypsum, is sold to a drywall manufacturer.
- The insulin plant produces yeast slurry as a fermentation byproduct. It's sold as animal feed to local pig farms.
- Wastewater is reused multiple times across the facilities before being treated.

The result: about 30+ exchanges of energy, water, and materials among ~20 different facilities, each one turning what would have been a waste output into a useful input.

> "None of these companies started out planning to be 'green.' They did it because the waste exchanges saved them money. The byproducts were free; using them instead of buying virgin materials cost less. The system grew organically over 50 years."

This is **industrial symbiosis** — the industrial equivalent of an ecosystem's food web. One company's output is another's input.

---

**2. Why It's Hard**

If industrial symbiosis is so good, why isn't it everywhere?

Real barriers:

- **Coordination.** It only works when multiple businesses share a location and are willing to coordinate. Standard supply chains span continents.
- **Specifications.** A factory's waste isn't exactly the form another factory needs. Often there's a small additional processing step required.
- **Contracts.** What if the producing factory closes? The consuming factory is now stuck.
- **Regulation.** In some jurisdictions, the rules around "waste" make it legally complicated to re-sell it as a product.
- **Capital.** Building pipes, conveyor belts, and infrastructure for cross-facility flows costs money upfront.

These are solvable problems. They are also reasons it doesn't happen by default.

> "Closing loops isn't 'free' — it requires upfront design and coordination work. The benefit comes later. Linear systems are easy to build; circular systems take more planning."

---

**3. The Smaller-Scale Examples**

You don't need an industrial park to have a closed loop. Smaller examples:

- **Backyard composting:** kitchen scraps → compost bin → garden soil → new food. Closed loop in your yard.
- **Refillable shampoo and detergent containers:** some stores now offer bulk refill stations where you bring your own bottle.
- **Library of Things:** in some cities, libraries lend tools, equipment, even kitchen appliances. One item, used by hundreds of people over its lifetime.
- **Repair cafés:** community spaces where volunteers help fix broken items instead of replacing them.
- **Second-hand stores, swap meets, online resale platforms:** extending product life by passing items to new users.

Each of these is a small loop-closing. None of them eliminate the linear API everywhere — but they reduce throughput in particular streams.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Identify a circular design example in your environment and continue terrarium observations.

### Activities

**1. Find a Closed Loop**

Look around your home, neighborhood, or community for one example of a circular design in action. Examples to look for:

- A compost bin (yours or a neighbor's)
- A library
- A second-hand store
- A return-and-refill shop
- A friend or family member who reuses or repurposes things creatively
- A school program that recycles or reuses materials

Write down what's circular about it. How does the loop close? What's the input, what's the output, and how do they connect?

---

**2. Spot the Almost-Circular**

Find one product or process in your environment that is *almost* circular but has one or two open points. What would need to change to close the loop fully?

Examples:

- A school recycling program where most things get recycled but some don't (e.g., plastic film, paper milk cartons). What's missing?
- A take-out restaurant that uses paper containers (compostable) but plastic-coated paper cups (mixed material). How could the cups be redesigned?
- A package from an online retailer that uses recyclable cardboard but ships with plastic bubble wrap inside. What would close the inner loop?

---

**3. Terrarium Check**

Week 9 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants — any changes?
- Reflect: the terrarium is one of the most efficient circular systems you can build. Its plants are biological nutrients to its decomposers, which produce biological nutrients to the next round of plants. The water cycle is closed. The CO₂ and O₂ cycles are closed. It's the design template.

### Systems Log

> **Today's System:** Circular design
>
> **Biological vs. technical nutrient sort:** [describe what you sorted]
>
> **A circular loop I found in real life:** ____
>
> **An almost-circular system that could be redesigned:** ____
>
> **Terrarium update:** ____

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "This object can go back to nature because ___."
- "This object could be remelted and reused because ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Aluminum cans recycle beautifully. Why hasn't aluminum become the dominant packaging material? (Hint: energy cost, cost per unit, weight.)
- What does "designed for the cradle-to-grave model" mean in everyday products? Look at a product label or warranty — does it tell you how to return materials to the system, or only how to throw them out?
- If a product is designed cradle-to-cradle but no one collects it back, does it still function as a circular product? What's needed in the surrounding infrastructure?

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Look at the items you sorted today. Most of them weren't designed to fit cleanly into either nutrient stream. Whose 'fault' is that?"

Trick question. It's not anyone's fault. The system that produced those items was optimized for low cost and fast manufacturing, not for closed loops. The designers, the manufacturers, and the customers were all responding to the same incentives — none of which rewarded loop-closing.

> "The redesign work isn't moral work. It's engineering work. Figure out which nutrient stream a material belongs in, design products to keep them separate, and build the infrastructure to collect them back."

**This week's takeaway:** Closing loops requires three things together — material clarity (biological vs. technical), product design (modular, separable, identifiable), and collection infrastructure (return paths). All three have to exist. Any one missing breaks the loop.
:::
