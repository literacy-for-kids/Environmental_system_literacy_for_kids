# Week 11: Why Human Systems Break Natural Ones
*Unit 4: Re-Engineering the Interface*

For ten weeks we have been building up vocabulary. This week we put it together into a single diagnostic.

Natural systems and industrial systems both move matter and energy. The difference is in their **API** — the pattern that defines how inputs and outputs connect.

- **Natural systems run on a circular API.** The output of every process becomes the input of another process. A leaf falls, decomposers break it down, the nutrients feed the next leaf. There is no "waste" — only inputs that haven't been picked up yet.

- **Industrial systems mostly run on a linear API.** Raw material is extracted from one place, manufactured into a product, used, and discarded into another place. The discard step has no return path. Outputs accumulate as waste.

Most environmental problems we have studied so far are versions of this single mismatch. Carbon piling up in the atmosphere (Week 5). Nitrogen running off into the ocean (Week 6). Microplastics in landfills (Week 7). Fisheries collapsing (Week 10). All the same pattern: a linear API outputting into a closed-loop system that isn't equipped to absorb it.

This week we name the pattern and look at what would change if we re-engineered the API.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- The big idea: most environmental problems share the same shape. Once students see the shape, they can spot it everywhere.
- "Linear" vs. "circular" API is a clean reframing. Avoid moralizing — both are valid system designs in some contexts; the question is the fit between them.
- This week sets up Weeks 12–14 (circular design, right-to-repair, redesign).
- Students may find the "API" framing technical. It's okay to use simpler language: "the connectors between systems," "the way matter flows in and out."
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "Nature reuses everything in circles. People mostly use things in straight lines — take, make, use, throw away. The trash has nowhere to go because nature wasn't built to handle it."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip the "API" term entirely. Use "the loop" or "how it connects."
- Skip industrial examples that require background knowledge. Use household examples.

**Adapting the activities:**
- Draw circles for natural cycles, straight arrows for human-made ones.

**Journal alternatives:** A drawing of a circular nature loop on one side of the page, and a straight-line human loop on the other side.

**What success looks like:** The learner can point to something and say whether it goes in a circle (like food in a garden) or in a straight line (like a plastic toy).
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Discuss the parallels with software APIs: an API defines how two systems exchange data. A bad API mismatch breaks integrations. The natural/industrial API mismatch is structurally the same problem, just with atoms instead of data.
- Discuss the concept of an "externality" in economics — a cost that isn't paid by the producer or buyer, but borne by someone else. Most environmental problems are externalities, and most externalities are downstream effects of a linear API.
- Discuss why purely linear systems can grow rapidly in the short term (no overhead for closing loops) but accumulate hidden costs.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### The Two APIs
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- compare the structure of a natural circular system with an industrial linear system
- explain why circular systems are stable in the long run and linear systems accumulate waste
- describe their terrarium as a circular API in action

---

#### Activities

**1. Draw the Two Diagrams**

Draw a natural system on one side of a sheet of paper:

```
       ┌──── ATMOSPHERE ────┐
       ↓                    ↑
    PLANTS  →  ANIMALS  →  SOIL
       ↑                    │
       └────────────────────┘
```

Then draw a typical industrial system on the other side:

```
RAW MATERIAL → MANUFACTURE → PRODUCT → USE → WASTE
   (mine,       (factory,    (your    (you   (landfill,
    drill,       processing) bought   used   ocean,
    log)                     it)      it)    atmosphere)
                                              [NO RETURN]
```

Side by side, the difference is structural:

- Natural: every output goes back into an input. The loop is closed.
- Industrial: outputs leave the system. The loop is open.

Introduce the two names:

> "Natural systems run a **circular API**. Industrial systems run a **linear API**. (API just means 'the way things plug together'.) The first one keeps reusing matter. The second one keeps taking new matter in and dumping used matter out."

---

**2. The Terrarium as Demonstration**

Walk over to the terrarium (it's been running for 8 weeks now).

> "Look at this. It's been running for 2 months. Has any new soil come in? Any new water? Any new plant matter from outside? No. Whatever's inside is what it has. And it's still alive."

That is a circular API working. The plants grow and shed leaves. Microbes in the soil decompose the leaves. The decomposed material feeds the next round of plant growth. The water evaporates from leaves, condenses on the lid, drips back to the soil. Same atoms, used over and over.

> "Now imagine if we sealed up an entire industrial supply chain inside a terrarium. The plastic bottle factory, the use, and the disposal. Would it work? No — within weeks the inside would fill up with waste plastic, the air would run out of fresh oxygen, and the system would jam."

This is why the linear API is hard to operate inside a closed system (like Earth). Linear systems require an external dumping ground. Earth doesn't have one.

---

**3. Why Linear API Works Short-Term**

Acknowledge: linear systems are not stupid. They are *easier to build*.

- No need to design for return paths.
- No need to coordinate end-of-life.
- Costs are externalized (someone else deals with the waste).
- Easier to scale rapidly.

> "The linear industrial API has fueled enormous human progress. Cheap goods, fast manufacturing, abundance of stuff. The catch is that the 'easy' part comes from skipping the loop-closing work. That work doesn't go away — it gets pushed onto the environment to handle."

The cost is paid by:
- Future users of land/water/air (after they're degraded)
- People who live near the dumping ground
- The natural systems that have to absorb the load
- Eventually, the people running the system, when downstream effects come back to them

This is the **externality** in economic terms. An externality is a cost that doesn't show up on the bill — it's paid by someone or something else.

## Guided Session 2
### Spotting the Mismatch
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- identify whether a given system is using a linear or circular API
- pinpoint where the loop opens in a linear system
- recognize that the same engineering pattern explains many seemingly unrelated environmental problems

---

#### Activities

**1. Sort the Examples**

Make two columns: "Circular API" and "Linear API." Sort these systems:

| System | Linear or Circular? |
|--------|---------------------|
| A forest ecosystem | Circular — every output is consumed |
| A typical fast-food meal (delivered in single-use containers) | Linear — most outputs go to landfill |
| Composting in a backyard garden | Circular — food waste becomes soil amendment |
| Buying a new phone every two years | Linear — old phone becomes e-waste |
| A municipal water system that treats and reuses wastewater | Hybrid — partially circular for water, often linear for sludge |
| A library | Circular — book is used many times by different people |
| A bottle of single-use shampoo | Linear — bottle ends up in landfill or ocean |
| A glass milk bottle returned and refilled (old system) | Circular — bottle is reused dozens of times |
| A solar panel | Mostly circular for energy; linear for manufacturing waste and end-of-life |

Discussion: notice that the linear-vs-circular distinction sometimes applies to *parts* of a system. The same product can be circular in one dimension and linear in another.

---

**2. Spot the Mismatch in Your Audits**

Pull out the "Away" Audit from Week 7. Look at the items the student tracked. For each one, identify:

- Where the loop opens.
- What the "output" is, and where it goes.
- What natural system has to absorb it.
- Whether that natural system has the bandwidth to absorb it at the rate it's being produced.

This is the **diagnosis** that will lead into the capstone project. Students are learning to look at any process and identify *exactly where the linear-circular mismatch is*.

---

**3. The Pattern Recognition Exercise**

Walk through several seemingly unrelated environmental problems. For each, identify the same shape:

| Problem | Linear flow | What can't keep up |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| Atmospheric CO₂ accumulation | Fossil fuels extracted, burned, dumped into atmosphere | Carbon cycle absorption rate |
| Plastic in oceans | Petroleum extracted, plastic made, used briefly, dumped | Decomposition rate (essentially zero on human timescales) |
| Eutrophication of coastal waters | Fertilizer extracted/made, applied to crops, ran off into water | Nitrogen cycle absorption rate |
| Fishery collapse | Fish extracted, sold, consumed | Reproduction rate |
| Aquifer depletion | Water extracted, used (often distantly), didn't return | Recharge rate |
| E-waste accumulation | Minerals extracted, electronics built, used briefly, discarded | Recycling and decomposition rate (essentially zero) |
| Topsoil loss | Conventional agriculture extracts nutrients, ships food away, soil eroded | Soil formation rate (~1 cm per 100–1000 years) |

The pattern is unmistakable. They are all **rate mismatches between a linear extraction-and-output process and the natural absorption capacity of the system receiving the outputs**.

> "If you can spot this pattern, you can diagnose almost any environmental problem. And once you can diagnose it, you can start designing fixes — which is what we'll do in the next three weeks."

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Spot linear and circular APIs in your own environment and continue terrarium observations.

### Activities

**1. The API Audit**

Walk around your home, school, or neighborhood. Find five different systems and categorize each:

- Linear (open loop): inputs taken in, outputs sent away.
- Circular (closed loop): outputs become inputs to other parts of the same system.

For each, identify where the loop opens or closes.

Examples:

- **Family kitchen:** food in, food scraps and packaging out → linear.
- **Vegetable garden with compost bin:** food scraps in, compost out → circular (for the food scraps).
- **Bathroom:** water in (clean), water out (used) → linear at your level (but the water goes to a treatment plant and re-enters the cycle).
- **A library:** books circulate among users → circular.
- **A clothing store:** new clothes arrive, sold to customers, customers later throw them out → linear.

---

**2. Pick One to Redesign**

Pick one linear system from your audit and sketch what it would look like as a circular system. Don't worry about whether it's practical yet — we will get into feasibility in the next three weeks. Just imagine the loop closing.

Examples:

- A bathroom system where greywater (shower, sink) is captured and used to flush toilets or water plants.
- A school lunch system where food scraps go into a school compost pile that feeds the school garden.
- A clothing system where worn-out clothes are returned to the store for fabric recycling.

---

**3. Terrarium Check**

Week 8 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants — any changes?
- Reflect: the terrarium is a working example of the circular API. It's now 8 weeks in, and it has produced essentially zero "waste." Everything that gets used gets reused. That's the model.

### Systems Log

> **Today's System:** Linear vs. circular APIs
>
> **Linear API diagram:** [draw it]
>
> **Circular API diagram:** [draw it]
>
> **A linear system in my home:** ____
>
> **A circular system in my home:** ____
>
> **Pattern I see across multiple environmental problems:** ____
>
> **Terrarium update:** ____

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "A circle system reuses ___."
- "A straight-line system needs ___ and produces ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Which is more "natural" — a linear or a circular API? Why?
- Are humans the only species that uses a linear API? (Look up: hardly anyone else does. Mostly because everyone else operates within natural decomposition loops by default.)
- A factory that operates purely linearly is often *cheaper* in the short term than one that operates circularly. Why? Who pays the difference?
- Can a single product be both linear and circular at the same time, in different dimensions?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Distinguish the two APIs:** "What's the difference between a linear system and a circular system?" (Looking for: linear has no return path; circular reuses outputs.)
2. **Identify a mismatch:** "Pick something in this room and explain whether it's linear or circular. Where does the loop open or close?" (Looking for: a reasonable attempt to trace the flow.)
3. **Spot the pattern:** "Atmospheric CO₂, plastic in oceans, and fertilizer in lakes are all examples of the same pattern. What's the pattern?" (Looking for: linear systems outputting into natural systems at a rate the natural system can't absorb.)

If the learner can do at least 2 of these, they're ready for Week 12.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Now that you can see the linear-circular distinction, look at your everyday environment. Most of it is linear. That isn't a moral observation. It's an observation about the engineering choices that built our current systems."

Once you see the pattern, you cannot unsee it. The pattern itself is the diagnostic tool.

> "What you do with that diagnostic is up to you. But you can never again look at a discarded bottle, a smokestack, a fertilizer runoff stream, and not see the same shape."

**This week's takeaway:** The environmental crisis is mostly one engineering pattern, repeated at many scales: linear industrial systems outputting into natural systems whose absorption capacity has been exceeded.
:::

- **From Week 3 (terrarium):** The terrarium is a working circular API. Closed for matter, open for energy.
- **From Week 5 (carbon) and Week 6 (nitrogen):** Both cycles can be diagrammed as circular APIs being overloaded by linear inputs (fossil fuels, Haber-Bosch).

:::tip Simplify (Ages 8–9)
Use only the two diagrams (circle and straight line). Skip the term "API." The takeaway: "Nature uses things over and over in circles. Most human-made stuff goes in a straight line and ends in the trash. The trash has to go somewhere, and nature can't handle as much as we make."
:::

:::tip Extend (Ages 10–12)
Look up the **circular economy** concept (the Ellen MacArthur Foundation is a clear source). Discuss the three principles: (1) eliminate waste and pollution by design, (2) keep products and materials in use, (3) regenerate natural systems. This is the same engineering frame you've been building, formalized as policy.
