# Environmental Systems Literacy Glossary

All the key terms from this curriculum, in plain language. Terms are listed alphabetically. The **Introduced** column shows where each word first appears.

---

| Term | Definition | Introduced |
|---|---|---|
| **Albedo** | How much sunlight a surface reflects rather than absorbs. White surfaces (snow, ice, clouds) have high albedo; dark surfaces (oceans, soil, asphalt) absorb more energy. Changes in albedo can amplify warming or cooling. | Optional Week 1 |
| **Amplifying loop** | A feedback loop where a change in the system causes more of the same change — pushing the system further from steady state. Example: ice melts → less sunlight reflected → more heat absorbed → more ice melts. | Week 9 |
| **Balancing loop** | A feedback loop where a change in the system triggers a response that pushes back toward steady state. Example: predators eat more prey when prey is abundant, which brings prey numbers back down. | Week 9 |
| **Biogeochemical cycle** | The natural circulation of an element (carbon, water, nitrogen) through the atmosphere, oceans, soil, and living organisms. These cycles are how matter gets reused on Earth. | Week 4 |
| **Biological nutrient** | A material that can safely re-enter biological cycles after use — like wood, paper, or food scraps. Biological nutrients are designed to compost back into the soil. | Week 12 |
| **Carbon cycle** | The biogeochemical cycle that moves carbon between the atmosphere, oceans, soil, biomass, and geological reservoirs. Photosynthesis pulls carbon out of the air; respiration and combustion put it back. | Week 5 |
| **Carrying capacity** | The maximum load (number of organisms, amount of harvest, level of pollution) that an environment can support indefinitely without degrading. Going over carrying capacity damages the system. | Week 8 |
| **Circular API** | A system where the outputs of each process become inputs to other processes. Natural ecosystems run on a circular API — waste from one organism is food for another. | Week 11 |
| **Closed loop** | A system in which matter cycles back to its starting point and is used again. The opposite of an open loop, where matter leaves the system as waste. | Week 3 |
| **Conservation of matter** | The physical law that matter cannot be created or destroyed — only moved or changed in form. Nothing actually goes "away"; it just changes location or state. | Week 7 |
| **Cradle-to-cradle** | A design philosophy where a product is planned from the start so that, at the end of its life, every material becomes an input to another product or cycle — never waste. | Week 12 |
| **Denitrification** | The microbial process that converts nitrogen compounds in the soil back into nitrogen gas (N₂), returning it to the atmosphere. The closing step of the nitrogen cycle. | Week 6 |
| **Designed obsolescence** | The deliberate practice of designing a product so that it stops working, becomes outdated, or cannot be repaired after a short period — pushing the user to buy a replacement. | Week 13 |
| **Direct air capture (DAC)** | A geoengineering technology that uses machines to pull carbon dioxide directly out of the atmosphere for storage. Currently energy-intensive and expensive. | Optional Week 2 |
| **Entropy** | A measure of how disordered or unusable the energy in a system is. Every energy conversion increases entropy somewhere — useful energy always degrades into less useful forms like waste heat. | Week 2 |
| **Eutrophication** | What happens when too much nitrogen or phosphorus enters a body of water — usually from fertilizer runoff. Algae bloom, die, decompose, and consume the oxygen, creating dead zones. | Week 6 |
| **Evaporation** | The process by which liquid water turns into water vapor and enters the atmosphere. Driven by solar energy. The starting step of the water cycle. | Week 4 |
| **Fossil fuel** | Coal, oil, or natural gas — geological reservoirs of carbon that took hundreds of millions of years to form. When burned, they release that stored carbon into the atmosphere in a tiny fraction of the time. | Week 5 |
| **Geoengineering** | Intentional, large-scale technical intervention in the planetary system — for example, reflecting more sunlight or pulling carbon out of the air. Treated in this curriculum as engineering tradeoffs, not advocacy. | Optional Week 2 |
| **Haber-Bosch process** | The industrial process that converts atmospheric nitrogen gas into ammonia using high pressure and heat. It is the human override of natural nitrogen fixation and supplies most agricultural fertilizer worldwide. | Week 6 |
| **Hydrological cycle** | Another name for the water cycle — the continuous movement of water through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, and groundwater. | Week 4 |
| **Ice-albedo feedback** | An amplifying loop where melting ice exposes darker land or ocean, which absorbs more solar energy, which warms the area further, which melts more ice. | Optional Week 1 |
| **Industrial symbiosis** | When one factory's output becomes another factory's input — closing a loop across multiple businesses. A real-world example of circular system design. | Week 12 |
| **Linear API** | A system organized as extract → manufacture → use → discard, with no return path for the discarded output. Most industrial systems run on a linear API. | Week 11 |
| **Maximum sustainable yield (MSY)** | The largest amount that can be taken from a renewable resource each cycle without depleting it. For a resource that regenerates at 25% per year, the MSY is 25% of the current stock. | Week 10 |
| **Microplastic** | A small fragment of plastic less than 5 millimeters across, produced as larger plastic items break down. Microplastics persist in soil, water, and food chains. | Week 7 |
| **Modular design** | A product design approach where the product is built from interchangeable parts that can be replaced or upgraded individually, rather than requiring the whole product to be thrown out when one part fails. | Week 13 |
| **Nitrogen fixation** | The process by which nitrogen gas (N₂) is converted into nitrogen compounds that plants can use. Done naturally by certain bacteria and industrially by the Haber-Bosch process. | Week 6 |
| **Open loop** | A system in which matter flows in one direction — input → process → discarded output — with no return path. Open loops accumulate waste outside the system. | Week 3 |
| **Overshoot** | When a population or industrial load exceeds the carrying capacity of its system. Usually followed by a crash as the system degrades and can no longer support the load. | Week 9 |
| **Photosynthesis** | The biological process by which plants use solar energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into sugar and oxygen. The base of nearly all food webs on Earth. | Week 1 |
| **Predator-prey cycle** | A balancing loop between two populations: more prey allows more predators, but more predators reduce prey, which reduces predators, which allows prey to recover. Often produces oscillating populations. | Week 9 |
| **Right-to-repair** | The principle that users should be able to repair the products they own — with access to parts, manuals, and tools — rather than being forced to replace them. A throughput-reduction strategy. | Week 13 |
| **Runoff** | Water from rain or snowmelt that flows across the surface of the land rather than soaking in. Picks up everything in its path — soil, fertilizer, pollutants — and carries it downstream. | Week 4 |
| **Steady state** | A condition where the inputs and outputs of a system are balanced, so the overall amount stays roughly the same over time. Ecosystems in steady state are not changing rapidly. | Week 8 |
| **Stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI)** | A proposed geoengineering technique: spray reflective particles into the stratosphere to reflect a small fraction of incoming sunlight, cooling the planet. Mimics the cooling effect of large volcanic eruptions. | Optional Week 2 |
| **Sustainable yield** | An amount that can be harvested from a renewable resource each cycle indefinitely without depleting it. Equal to or less than the regeneration rate. | Week 10 |
| **Systems Log** | The student journal used throughout this curriculum. A record of observations, system diagrams, calculations, diagnoses, and reflections. | Week 1 |
| **Technical nutrient** | A material that can be reused indefinitely in industrial cycles — like aluminum, steel, or glass — provided it is collected and reprocessed properly. The industrial counterpart to a biological nutrient. | Week 12 |
| **Termination shock** | The sudden rebound effect that would occur if a geoengineering intervention (like SAI) were stopped — accumulated warming would reassert itself rapidly. | Optional Week 2 |
| **Thermodynamics, First Law** | Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred or converted from one form to another. | Week 2 |
| **Thermodynamics, Second Law** | Every energy conversion increases the total entropy of the system. Useful energy always degrades into less useful forms, especially low-grade heat. | Week 2 |
| **Throughput** | The total flow of matter and energy through a system per unit time. Reducing throughput (using less, lasting longer) reduces load on both the input and output sides. | Week 11 |
| **Tipping point** | A threshold in a system beyond which the dominant feedback loop flips from balancing to amplifying — making the change self-sustaining and hard to reverse. | Optional Week 1 |
| **Tragedy of the commons** | What happens when many users share a single resource pool and each user benefits from taking more, even though collective over-harvesting destroys the pool for everyone. | Week 10 |
| **Transpiration** | The process by which plants pull water up from the soil and release it as vapor through their leaves. A major contributor to the water cycle. | Week 4 |
| **Trophic efficiency** | The fraction of energy that passes from one level of a food web to the next. Typically about 10% — the rest is lost as heat or used by the organism for living. | Week 2 |
