# Optional Week 2: Intentional Planetary-Scale Interventions
*Optional Extension — Geoengineering as Systems Analysis*

This is an optional extension week. The topic is **geoengineering** — deliberate, large-scale technological interventions intended to counteract climate change.

The framing of this week is critical: **pure systems analysis, no advocacy.** The student is acting as an engineer evaluating proposed solutions, not as an advocate for or against any of them. Each intervention gets the same treatment: how is it supposed to work, what's the intended effect, and what are the second-order effects and failure modes?

Some of these interventions are decades away from being viable. Some are being researched right now. None of them are unambiguously good or bad — they all involve serious tradeoffs and uncertainties. The goal is to think about them the way you'd think about any engineering proposal: what's the system, what would the patch do, and what could go wrong?

The course's overall position, by the end: **these are patches, not fixes.** They address symptoms (excess heat, excess CO₂), not root causes (the linear/circular API mismatch). A patch can buy time. It doesn't change the underlying architecture. And some patches introduce new failure modes that may be worse than what they were trying to solve.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- This is the most politically charged topic in the curriculum. Stay neutral. Engineering analysis only.
- Three categories of intervention: Solar Radiation Management (SRM), Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR), and Enhanced Weathering.
- For each one, the student maps the intended mechanism and identifies 2–3 potential second-order effects or failure modes.
- The final framing: these are patches, not fixes. They can buy time. They don't change the linear/circular API mismatch.

## Facilitator Preparation

:::info Before You Begin
- Read through the three intervention categories ahead of time. The science is real but complicated; being familiar with each helps facilitation.
- Be ready to push back on advocacy in either direction. If the student says "this is amazing, we should do it," ask about failure modes. If they say "this is terrible, we should ban it," ask what the alternative is.
- The Mount Pinatubo example (1991 volcanic cooling) is a useful natural analog for stratospheric aerosol injection.
- Pull the Systems Log.
:::

:::tip Facilitation Mindset
**Neutrality is the lesson, not just the technique.** The student isn't being trained to support or oppose geoengineering. They're being trained to *think* about geoengineering as engineers think about any large intervention: what's the mechanism, what's the intended effect, what could go wrong, what does it not solve?

**"Patch, not fix" is the framing the week builds toward.** Don't impose this conclusion upfront. Let the analysis lead the student there.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "Some scientists are thinking about really big ideas to slow down climate change — like making clouds reflect more sunlight, or building giant machines that pull CO₂ out of the air. We're going to look at three big ideas and think about what could go right and what could go wrong with each one."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip ocean iron fertilization and enhanced weathering. Focus only on SAI (the "sulfur clouds" idea) and DAC (the "CO₂ vacuum" idea).
- Skip termination shock, moral hazard, and governance complications.
- Skip the system-diagram activity. Replace with: "Draw a picture of one of the ideas. Then draw two arrows showing 'good things that could happen' and 'bad things that could happen.'"

**Adapting the activities:**
- Use the Mount Pinatubo example concretely: "A volcano erupted, lots of dust got into the sky, and the whole planet got a little cooler for a year or two. Some scientists wonder if we could do that on purpose."
- Keep the discussion concrete: "If you sneezed and made everyone in your room cooler, but you couldn't stop sneezing without everyone suddenly getting hot again, would you start sneezing in the first place?"

**Journal alternatives:** "Pick one big idea. Draw it. Write one sentence about what's good and one sentence about what's risky."

**What success looks like:** The learner can describe one geoengineering proposal and name one thing that could go wrong with it.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- All three intervention categories covered.
- The full system-diagram activity for each.
- Discuss governance: who decides whether to deploy a global thermostat? What are the political consequences of unilateral deployment?
- Discuss moral hazard explicitly: does the existence of a "fix" reduce pressure to address the underlying linear API?
:::

## Guided Session 1
### Solar Radiation Management (SRM)
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- describe Solar Radiation Management as a category of intervention
- explain the proposed mechanism for stratospheric aerosol injection
- identify at least two failure modes or second-order effects, including termination shock

---

#### Activities

**1. The Three Categories Overview**

Geoengineering proposals fall into three broad categories:

| Category | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Solar Radiation Management (SRM)** | Reduces the amount of solar energy entering the system | Stratospheric aerosol injection, marine cloud brightening |
| **Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR)** | Removes CO₂ from the atmosphere | Direct air capture, ocean iron fertilization, large-scale reforestation |
| **Enhanced Weathering** | Accelerates natural CO₂-absorption processes | Spreading ground-up silicate rock on land or oceans |

SRM works on the energy side of the climate equation. CDR and enhanced weathering work on the carbon side. The two address completely different parts of the problem.

---

**2. Solar Radiation Management — How It Works**

The basic idea: the Earth absorbs solar energy and emits some of it back as heat. If less solar energy enters the system in the first place, less heat builds up. SRM proposals aim to *reflect more sunlight back to space* before it reaches the surface.

The most-discussed SRM technique is **Stratospheric Aerosol Injection (SAI)**:

- Inject reflective particles (sulfur dioxide, or possibly calcium carbonate) into the stratosphere — roughly 20 km up.
- The particles spread into a thin global layer that reflects a small fraction of incoming sunlight.
- The result: lower average surface temperature.

The intended mechanism diagram:

```
   [Sun] → [solar energy in]
              ↓
       [stratospheric particles] → [reflects some sunlight back to space]
              ↓
       [less sunlight reaches surface]
              ↓
       [lower surface temperature]
```

---

**3. The Natural Analog: Mount Pinatubo**

In 1991, Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines erupted and injected approximately 20 million tonnes of sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere. The sulfur dioxide spread globally and formed a reflective haze.

Result: global average surface temperatures dropped by roughly **0.5°C for about 1–2 years**, before the particles settled out of the stratosphere and the cooling effect faded.

This is the existence proof that stratospheric aerosols can cool the planet. The volcanic eruption did it accidentally. The proposal is to do it intentionally and continuously — replenishing the aerosols as they settle out.

---

**4. Failure Modes and Second-Order Effects**

SAI has several serious engineering concerns:

**Termination Shock.**

The particles only stay in the stratosphere for ~1–2 years. To maintain cooling, you have to keep injecting. If injection stops suddenly — political crisis, war, equipment failure — the particles wash out and surface temperatures *snap back up to where they would have been*. Potentially several degrees of warming compressed into a year or two.

Climate ecosystems and human infrastructure can adapt slowly to gradual warming. They cannot adapt to a sudden snap-back. Termination shock could cause more damage than the original gradual warming would have.

This is the most cited failure mode of SAI. Once you start, stopping becomes much more dangerous than continuing.

**Unequal Regional Effects.**

The cooling from SAI is not uniform. Different regions see different temperature and precipitation changes. Modeling suggests that some regions (notably parts of the tropics) could see significant disruption to monsoon patterns and rainfall — even as the global average temperature drops.

Whoever benefits and whoever suffers from SAI deployment is a political question, not just a technical one. The "global thermostat" doesn't have one setting that works for everyone.

**Governance Problem.**

Who decides whether to deploy SAI? No country owns the stratosphere. A single country (or even a single very wealthy individual) could attempt deployment unilaterally. This creates a governance problem of a kind no current institution is designed to handle.

The technology may be deployable before the political framework for deciding *whether* to deploy it exists.

**Moral Hazard.**

Critics argue: if a cheap technological fix appears available, the political pressure to reduce emissions weakens. The presence of the "patch" reduces incentive to solve the root cause.

Supporters argue: SAI buys time for the slower work of decarbonization. The two are complements, not substitutes.

Both positions are real engineering concerns. The student doesn't need to pick a side. They need to understand that the existence of the patch changes the political and economic system around it.

**Doesn't Address the Root Cause.**

SAI cools the surface. It does not reduce CO₂. Atmospheric CO₂ continues to rise as long as emissions continue. Ocean acidification — a direct effect of CO₂ dissolving in seawater, independent of temperature — continues unchecked. Many other effects of high CO₂ persist.

SAI is, at best, a symptom-masking treatment. The disease — the open-loop fossil-carbon system — is unchanged.

---

**5. The Engineering Verdict on SRM**

The student writes a one-paragraph engineering assessment of SAI:

- What it would do (best case)
- What it would not do
- The most serious failure mode
- The most serious unsolved problem

No advocacy. Just analysis.

## Guided Session 2
### Carbon Dioxide Removal and Enhanced Weathering
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- describe Carbon Dioxide Removal as a category of intervention
- evaluate direct air capture and ocean iron fertilization on engineering merits
- describe enhanced weathering as a separate intervention category

---

#### Activities

**1. Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR) — The Concept**

Unlike SRM, CDR addresses the root cause: it removes CO₂ from the atmosphere directly. If enough CO₂ is removed, the greenhouse effect weakens, and warming slows or reverses.

CDR is more "honest" than SRM in the sense that it works on the underlying system (carbon load), not just the surface effect (temperature). But CDR has its own engineering challenges, mostly around scale and energy cost.

---

**2. Direct Air Capture (DAC)**

The mechanism:

- Large mechanical systems pull ambient air through a sorbent material that selectively absorbs CO₂.
- Once the sorbent is saturated, it's heated or treated to release the captured CO₂ in concentrated form.
- The concentrated CO₂ is then either used industrially or pumped underground for permanent geological storage.

The intended diagram:

```
   [Ambient air, ~420 ppm CO₂] → [DAC unit] → [Air with CO₂ removed]
                                       ↓
                              [Concentrated CO₂]
                                       ↓
                              [Geological storage or industrial use]
```

**Engineering challenges:**

**Energy Cost.** Current DAC technology requires roughly **2,000 kWh per tonne of CO₂ removed** (estimates vary by system; some advanced designs claim less). To put that in perspective: that's the equivalent of running a typical home for two months to remove one tonne of CO₂.

Annual human CO₂ emissions are roughly **40 billion tonnes**. To offset even 1 billion tonnes per year using DAC at current energy costs would require approximately **2 trillion kWh per year** — equivalent to the output of hundreds of large power plants.

**Where Does the Energy Come From?** This is the killer constraint. If DAC runs on fossil-fueled electricity, you might emit more CO₂ generating the power than you remove from the air. The system has to run on low-carbon energy (renewable, nuclear) to make sense. So DAC scaling depends on first scaling clean energy by even more than DAC itself.

**Permanent Storage.** Captured CO₂ has to go somewhere — typically pumped into deep geological formations (saline aquifers, depleted oil/gas fields). This requires:
- Suitable geology (not available everywhere)
- Long-term monitoring (the CO₂ must stay underground for centuries to count as "removed")
- Pipeline infrastructure to move CO₂ from capture sites to storage sites

**Cost per Tonne.** Current estimates for DAC range from roughly $300–1,000 per tonne of CO₂ removed. To meaningfully impact climate, the cost would need to drop dramatically, or massive public funding would be required.

**Engineering verdict on DAC:** real technology, real potential, but the energy and cost constraints mean it can't scale to a significant fraction of global emissions without enormous infrastructure buildout. It is much more expensive than not emitting in the first place.

---

**3. Ocean Iron Fertilization**

The mechanism:

- Many ocean regions are nutrient-rich except for iron, which limits the growth of phytoplankton (microscopic plants).
- Adding iron triggers a **phytoplankton bloom** — rapid growth of these plants.
- The phytoplankton absorb CO₂ from surface waters (which in turn absorb more from the atmosphere) as they grow.
- When the phytoplankton die, some of them sink to the deep ocean, carrying the carbon with them. If the carbon stays in the deep ocean for centuries, it's effectively removed from the atmosphere.

The diagram:

```
   [Add iron to ocean] → [phytoplankton bloom] → [absorb CO₂]
                                                    ↓
                                          [phytoplankton die]
                                                    ↓
                                          [some sink to deep ocean]
                                                    ↓
                                          [carbon stays in deep ocean]
```

**Engineering challenges:**

**Limited Effectiveness.** Field experiments have shown that ocean iron fertilization does trigger blooms, but the fraction of carbon that *actually* sinks to deep storage (versus being recycled in surface waters) appears to be lower than initial proposals assumed.

**Oxygen Depletion.** As the phytoplankton sink and decompose, bacteria consume oxygen. Large-scale fertilization could create deep-ocean dead zones — areas with too little oxygen to support marine life.

**Ecosystem Disruption.** Triggering large blooms changes which species dominate the food web. The downstream effects on fisheries, marine mammals, and nutrient cycling are hard to predict.

**Engineering verdict on ocean iron fertilization:** plausible mechanism, but real-world tests have underperformed expectations, and the second-order effects on marine ecosystems are not well-characterized. Not currently considered a leading candidate.

---

**4. Enhanced Weathering**

The mechanism is borrowed from geology. Natural silicate rocks (basalt, olivine) slowly absorb CO₂ as they weather — a chemical reaction that produces stable carbonate minerals. This is part of the slow geological carbon cycle, operating on timescales of millions of years.

Enhanced weathering accelerates this by:

- Mining silicate rock
- Grinding it into fine powder (more surface area = faster reaction)
- Spreading it on agricultural fields, beaches, or oceans
- Letting the increased surface area and contact with rain accelerate CO₂ absorption

```
   [Mine silicate rock] → [grind to fine powder]
                                ↓
                          [spread on land/ocean]
                                ↓
                       [accelerated weathering]
                                ↓
                          [CO₂ absorbed into rock]
                                ↓
                       [stable carbonate minerals]
```

**Engineering challenges:**

**Scale.** To absorb meaningful amounts of CO₂ would require mining and grinding billions of tonnes of rock per year — comparable in scale to global coal mining.

**Energy Cost of Grinding.** Grinding rock to a fine powder takes substantial energy. Some of the CO₂ removed by the weathering would be re-emitted by the grinding equipment unless the equipment runs on clean energy.

**Soil and Water Chemistry Effects.** Spreading silicate rock dust changes the chemistry of soils and waters. Some changes could be beneficial (some agricultural soils benefit from added silicates). Some could be harmful (heavy metals in some rock sources, pH shifts, dust exposure).

**Engineering verdict on enhanced weathering:** plausible mechanism, real research underway, but unproven at scale and with significant logistical challenges. Likely a small contributor, not a primary solution.

---

**5. The "Patch vs. Fix" Framing**

Pull all three categories together:

| Intervention | Addresses | Doesn't address |
|---|---|---|
| SRM (SAI, etc.) | Symptom: surface temperature | Root cause: CO₂ load; ocean acidification |
| CDR (DAC, etc.) | Root cause: atmospheric CO₂ | The flow of new emissions; cost/energy constraints |
| Enhanced weathering | Root cause: atmospheric CO₂ | Scale; logistics; soil effects |

All three are interventions on a system that *continues to generate the problem* through the linear API. As long as fossil carbon keeps flowing from the ground into the atmosphere, geoengineering is fighting a flow with patches.

The engineering observation:

> "A patch can buy time. It doesn't change the underlying architecture. And some patches introduce new failure modes."

> "The most reliable fix is to close the open loop at its source — keep fossil carbon in the ground, recycle materials, redesign the linear API into a circular one. Geoengineering is what you reach for when you've run out of time to do the harder work."

This isn't an argument against geoengineering. It's a structural observation about what geoengineering can and can't do.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Build system diagrams for all three intervention categories and write a brief engineering assessment.

### Activities

**1. The Three System Diagrams**

For each intervention category, the student creates a system diagram showing:

- The proposed mechanism (how it's supposed to work)
- The intended effect (what changes)
- 2–3 potential second-order effects or failure modes (what else changes, or what could go wrong)

Template:

```
INTERVENTION: ___________________

PROPOSED MECHANISM:
[Diagram or 2-3 sentence description]

INTENDED EFFECT:
[1 sentence]

SECOND-ORDER EFFECTS / FAILURE MODES:
1. _____
2. _____
3. _____

ENGINEERING ASSESSMENT (1 paragraph):
[Neutral analysis: real, plausible, scalable, addresses root cause or symptom only]
```

Do this once for SRM (SAI), once for CDR (DAC), and once for enhanced weathering.

---

**2. The Comparison Table**

Once all three diagrams are complete, the student fills in a comparison:

```
INTERVENTION COMPARISON

                            SRM (SAI)   CDR (DAC)   Enhanced Weathering
Addresses CO₂?              No          Yes         Yes
Addresses temperature?      Yes         Yes (slow)  Yes (slow)
Reversible if stopped?      No (term.   Yes         Yes
                            shock)
Energy cost?                Low         Very high   Moderate
Scale today?                Research    Tiny        Research
Major second-order risk?    Term. shock Energy use  Soil/water effects
                            unequal     scale       chemistry
                            effects
Root cause or patch?        Patch       Patch       Patch
                            (symptom)   (downstream)(downstream)
```

(The student fills in their own version, may differ in specifics.)

---

**3. The Verdict Paragraph**

The student writes a closing paragraph: their engineering assessment of the geoengineering landscape as a whole.

Suggested prompts:

- Which intervention seems most promising on engineering grounds, and why?
- Which seems most dangerous, and why?
- Where does geoengineering fit into the curriculum's overall framing of the environmental system?
- Does the existence of these interventions change how you think about the capstone proposal you worked on?

The student is not required to take a position. They are required to think clearly about the engineering tradeoffs.

### Reflection Questions

- Why is termination shock such a critical failure mode for SAI? What would a system without termination shock look like?
- DAC could remove CO₂ permanently, but at enormous cost. Is there a price at which DAC starts to look attractive compared to other options?
- The "patch vs. fix" distinction matters because patches don't change the underlying system. Does it matter, in practice, whether a solution is a patch or a fix — if the patch works?
- If you were on a committee deciding whether your country should fund geoengineering research, what questions would you want answered first?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Categorize an intervention:** "Is enhanced weathering SRM or CDR? Why?" (Looking for: CDR, because it addresses atmospheric CO₂, not solar input.)
2. **Name a failure mode:** "Describe termination shock and why it matters for SAI." (Looking for: if SAI is stopped suddenly, particles wash out and warming returns rapidly — potentially worse than no intervention.)
3. **Apply the patch/fix framing:** "Is DAC a patch or a fix? Why?" (Looking for: a patch on the symptom side, because it doesn't close the open-loop fossil carbon system at the source — but it does work on the root cause carbon load directly, making it a stronger patch than SRM.)

If the learner can do all 3, they understand geoengineering at a useful level.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Notice how the engineering analysis didn't tell you whether geoengineering is good or bad. It told you what each intervention does, what it doesn't do, and what could go wrong. That's the engineer's job."

The political and moral questions are separate. They depend on values — how much risk you're willing to accept, who you trust to deploy the technology, what tradeoffs you're willing to make. The engineering analysis informs those questions but doesn't answer them.

> "What the engineering analysis does say clearly: all three categories are *patches* on a system that continues to generate the problem. The original linear/circular API mismatch is still there. Geoengineering buys time. It doesn't redesign the system."

> "If you only spend your effort on patches, you may end up with a system that's increasingly dependent on patches that themselves can fail. The more reliable strategy is to combine: buy time with patches where necessary, but use that time to close the open loop at the source."

**This week's takeaway:** Geoengineering is real engineering, with real tradeoffs and real failure modes. It's not magic, and it's not evil. It's a category of patches on a system that needs deeper redesign. The patches and the redesign are complements, not substitutes.
:::

:::tip Connecting to Earlier Weeks
- **From Week 11 (the linear API):** Geoengineering doesn't close the linear API. It tries to manage the downstream consequences of leaving the API open.
- **From Week 5 (the carbon cycle):** DAC and enhanced weathering try to manually accelerate the slow geological carbon cycle. Whether that's feasible at scale is the open engineering question.

## Connection to the Course

This optional week extends the curriculum into one of the most contested areas of climate engineering. The course's overall framing — that the environmental crisis is a linear/circular API mismatch — applies to geoengineering directly. The proposed interventions are patches on the symptoms of the mismatch. None of them close the loop.

That doesn't make them useless. It makes them tools with specific use cases and specific failure modes. The engineer's job is to know which tool fits which problem — and to keep the underlying system in view even when reaching for a patch.
