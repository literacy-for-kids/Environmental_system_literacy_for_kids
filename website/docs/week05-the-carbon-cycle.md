# Week 5: How Carbon Moves Through Living Systems
*Unit 2: The Plumbing and the Supply Chain*

Last week we mapped the water cycle. This week we map a different cycle — but the engineering model is the same: reservoirs, flows between them, and the cycle's overall steady state.

Carbon is the element that makes up the structural backbone of every living thing. Your body, every tree, every fish, every microbe — all made of carbon-based molecules. Carbon is also a major component of the atmosphere (as CO₂), the ocean (dissolved CO₂ and carbonate minerals), the soil (in organic matter), and deep geological reservoirs (coal, oil, natural gas, limestone).

Carbon cycles between these reservoirs constantly. The cycle has been roughly in steady state for thousands of years — atmospheric carbon has stayed within a relatively narrow range.

But over the last ~200 years, humans have been moving carbon from a slow geological reservoir into the fast atmospheric reservoir at a rate the natural cycle was not designed to handle. That mismatch — fast input, slow removal — is what makes the carbon cycle the central engineering issue of our era.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- The big idea: carbon moves between reservoirs on very different timescales. Plants and the atmosphere trade carbon in days. Geological reservoirs (coal, oil) lock carbon away for hundreds of millions of years.
- The climate connection: burning fossil fuels takes carbon from the slow geological reservoir and dumps it into the fast atmospheric reservoir. The natural cycle takes thousands of years to absorb it back.
- Use engineering language: "the rate of input exceeds the rate of removal," not "we are destroying the climate."
- The science here is well-established. CO₂ traps infrared radiation; more CO₂ traps more heat. This is physics measured in labs for over 150 years.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "Carbon is the stuff plants and animals are made of. It keeps moving between the air, plants, animals, soil, and the ground. Right now, humans are taking some out of the ground really fast and putting it into the air."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip the formal reservoir flow numbers. Use simple language: "atmosphere," "plants and animals," "soil," and "deep in the ground."
- Skip the residence time discussion.

**Adapting the activities:**
- Draw the cycle with stick figures and big arrows. Make the arrows different thicknesses to show fast vs. slow flows.

**Journal alternatives:** Draw the carbon cycle. Use cartoon arrows for fast flows and squiggly slow arrows for the geological reservoir.

**What success looks like:** The learner can say "carbon moves between the air, plants, soil, and the ground" and explain that burning coal and gasoline moves carbon from the ground to the air.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Atmospheric CO₂ pre-industrial was about 280 parts per million (ppm). Today it is about 425 ppm. That is roughly a 50% increase.
- Photosynthesis pulls about 120 gigatonnes of carbon from the atmosphere into plants each year. Respiration and decomposition release a similar amount. The natural cycle is in rough balance.
- Human fossil fuel combustion adds about 10 gigatonnes of carbon per year on top of the natural cycle.
- About half of the added carbon is absorbed by the oceans and land vegetation. The other half stays in the atmosphere.
- Have students graph the famous Keeling Curve (atmospheric CO₂ measured continuously since 1958) to see the change.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### The Carbon Reservoirs
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- name the main reservoirs where carbon is stored
- describe the natural flows that move carbon between them
- recognize that the same atoms of carbon cycle continuously through living things

---

#### Activities

**1. Where Is Carbon?**

Ask the student:

> "I'm thinking of an element. Almost every living thing on Earth is built from it. There's a lot of it dissolved in the oceans. Plants pull it out of the air. We breathe it out every time we exhale. What is it?"

Carbon.

Now ask: where is most of the carbon on Earth stored?

| Reservoir | Approximate share of total Earth carbon |
|-----------|-----------------------------------------|
| **Sedimentary rocks** (limestone, etc.) | ~99.95% |
| **Ocean** (dissolved CO₂, carbonate) | ~0.038% |
| **Fossil fuels** (coal, oil, gas) | ~0.005% |
| **Soils and biomass** (plants, animals, dead matter) | ~0.005% |
| **Atmosphere** (CO₂, CH₄) | ~0.001% |

(Exact percentages depend on the source, but the point is the *order of magnitude*: nearly all of Earth's carbon is locked in rocks, not in the fast cycle.)

Discussion:

- The atmosphere has the smallest share but drives much of what we notice.
- Fossil fuels are a tiny fraction of total carbon — but they used to be in the soil/biomass reservoir, and we are moving them back into the atmosphere quickly.

---

**2. The Living Carbon Loop**

Walk through the day-to-day carbon cycle (the **fast cycle**):

1. **Plants** take CO₂ from the atmosphere via **photosynthesis**, combine it with water and sunlight, and build sugar and other carbon-based tissues.
2. **Animals** eat plants (or eat animals that ate plants), so the carbon moves into animal bodies.
3. **Respiration** (breathing): both plants and animals release CO₂ back to the atmosphere as a waste product of using the stored chemical energy in their food.
4. **Decomposition:** when plants and animals die, microbes break them down, releasing some carbon back to the atmosphere as CO₂ and storing some in the soil as organic matter.

Draw the loop. It's a circle. The same carbon atom might be in the air today, in a leaf next week, in a cow next month, in the soil next year, back in the air next decade.

> "This is the **fast carbon cycle**. The atoms keep moving. The total amount stays roughly the same."

---

**3. The Slow Loop**

There is also a **slow carbon cycle** that operates on geological timescales:

1. Over hundreds of millions of years, dead plants and marine organisms that get buried under sediment can be transformed by heat and pressure into **coal, oil, or natural gas**. Their carbon gets locked underground.
2. Slowly, plate tectonics and volcanic activity can release small amounts of geological carbon back to the atmosphere as CO₂.
3. Meanwhile, weathering of rocks slowly absorbs atmospheric CO₂ back into mineral form.

> "On geological timescales, this slow cycle keeps the atmospheric carbon level in roughly steady state."

The phrase to lock in: **"fast cycle" (years) versus "slow cycle" (hundreds of millions of years)**.

## Guided Session 2
### The Speed Mismatch
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- explain why burning fossil fuels is functionally moving carbon from the slow cycle to the fast cycle at high speed
- understand the greenhouse effect as a basic physics phenomenon
- frame the climate problem in engineering terms: rate of input exceeding rate of removal

---

#### Activities

**1. What Is a Fossil Fuel?**

Hold up a piece of coal (or just describe one). Ask:

> "What do you think coal actually is?"

The answer: **coal is the fossilized remains of plants that lived hundreds of millions of years ago.** When those plants photosynthesized, they pulled CO₂ out of the atmosphere. Some of them got buried and never decomposed. Over geological time, heat and pressure compressed them into a black carbon-rich rock.

Same story for **oil** and **natural gas**: ancient marine organisms (mostly algae and plankton) that got buried in ocean sediments and slowly turned into fossil hydrocarbons.

> "Every gallon of gasoline you put in a car is ancient sunlight, captured by ancient organisms, stored underground for tens of millions of years."

---

**2. The Rate Mismatch**

Now do the math. Approximate numbers:

- The fossil fuel reservoir took **~300 million years** to form.
- Humans have burned a significant fraction of it (especially the easy-to-reach parts) in **~250 years**.

That's roughly a **million-times speedup** in the rate at which carbon moves from the slow reservoir to the atmosphere.

> "Think about a leaky bathtub. The drain is open (natural absorption by oceans and plants). The faucet is the natural slow release from rocks and volcanoes — a trickle. The system is in balance. Now you turn on a fire hose into the tub at a million times the normal flow rate. The drain can't keep up. The tub fills up."

The "tub filling up" is the atmospheric CO₂ increase. From ~280 ppm before the industrial revolution to ~425 ppm today.

---

**3. Why It Warms**

Explain the **greenhouse effect** in three sentences:

1. The sun sends mostly visible light. Earth absorbs it and warms up.
2. Earth re-radiates that energy as infrared (heat). Some of it escapes to space.
3. Certain gases in the atmosphere — including CO₂, methane (CH₄), and water vapor — are transparent to incoming visible light but absorb outgoing infrared. They slow the rate at which heat escapes.

More CO₂ = more infrared absorbed = slower heat loss = warmer atmosphere on average.

> "This is the same physics as the glass roof of a greenhouse. Light gets in. Heat has trouble getting out. Even children's science museums have demonstrations of this — it's not in dispute as physics."

If a student asks "but how do we know it's the CO₂, not just natural variation?", a good honest answer is: scientists know the precise infrared absorption spectrum of CO₂ from lab measurements going back to the 1850s. They know how much CO₂ has been added (it's measured continuously). They can calculate the expected warming from that added CO₂. The observed warming matches the calculation reasonably well. Multiple independent lines of evidence (ice cores, ocean temperature, isotopes) all point the same direction.

What is contested politically is **what to do about it.** What is not contested scientifically is **that it is happening and why.**

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Trace carbon through real-world systems and continue terrarium observations.

### Activities

**1. Trace the Carbon**

For each of the following, trace where the carbon came from and where it goes:

| Item | Where carbon came from | Where it goes after use |
|------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| **A loaf of bread** | Wheat plant absorbed CO₂ from atmosphere | Eaten → respired back to atmosphere as CO₂ |
| **A piece of firewood** | Tree absorbed CO₂ over years/decades | Burned → returns to atmosphere as CO₂ in minutes |
| **A gallon of gasoline** | Ancient marine organisms (300+ million years ago) absorbed CO₂ | Burned → released to atmosphere as CO₂ in seconds |
| **A plastic toy** | Crude oil — see above | Discarded — see Week 7 |
| **A glass of cow's milk** | Grass absorbed CO₂ → cow ate grass | Drunk → respired back to atmosphere |

Notice the pattern: the same carbon keeps cycling. The difference is **how long it was locked up** before being released.

---

**2. The "Slow Versus Fast" Sort**

Make two columns: "Fast carbon" and "Slow carbon." Sort these items:

- A potato (fast — recent photosynthesis)
- A gallon of diesel fuel (slow — geological reservoir)
- A wooden table (fast — tree carbon)
- A plastic bag (slow — petroleum)
- Cardboard (fast — paper/tree)
- A bag of charcoal briquettes (fast — wood that was burned slowly)
- Concrete (mostly limestone — slow geological)

Discussion: when something is *fast* carbon, burning it just returns recently-captured carbon to the atmosphere. When something is *slow* carbon, burning it adds atmospheric carbon that was locked away.

---

**3. Terrarium Check**

Week 2 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants, anything new?
- This week, look at the plants. Are they greener, the same, or paler than last week? They are doing photosynthesis right now — pulling CO₂ from the air inside the jar and using it to grow.

### Systems Log

> **Today's System:** The carbon cycle
>
> **Sketch:** [Draw the fast carbon cycle (atmosphere ↔ plants ↔ animals ↔ soil) and the slow carbon cycle (geological reservoirs ↔ atmosphere)]
>
> **Three items I traced:**
> - ____
> - ____
> - ____
>
> **The system mismatch I noticed:** ____
>
> **Terrarium update:** ____

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "Carbon comes from ___ and goes to ___."
- "When we burn ___, carbon moves from ___ to ___."

### Reflection Questions

- A tree absorbs CO₂ for 50 years. Then we cut it down and burn it. How did that change the system? (Net effect: roughly zero — the recent carbon returns to the atmosphere it came from.)
- We dig up coal that was buried 300 million years ago and burn it. How did that change the system? (Net effect: added carbon to the fast cycle that was previously locked in the slow reservoir.)
- What would it take for the atmosphere's carbon level to drop back to its pre-industrial value? (Either reducing the input, increasing the absorption, or both. And waiting — the natural absorption is slow.)

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Name reservoirs:** "Where is carbon stored on Earth?" (Looking for: atmosphere, plants/animals, soil, ocean, geological — any three.)
2. **Describe the natural cycle:** "How does carbon get from the atmosphere into a tree?" (Looking for: photosynthesis or "the tree breathes it in.")
3. **Identify the mismatch:** "Why is burning coal different from burning wood, from the system's point of view?" (Looking for: coal releases ancient carbon that was locked away; wood releases recent carbon that was already in the cycle.)

If the learner can do at least 2 of these, they're ready for Week 6.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask the student:

> "The atmosphere doesn't care where the carbon came from. A CO₂ molecule is a CO₂ molecule. What matters to the system is the rate at which they're being added versus the rate they're being removed."

This is the engineer's view of climate change. Not a moral story about villains. A system with a mismatch between two flow rates.

**This week's takeaway:** Carbon cycles between reservoirs at vastly different speeds. Move carbon from a slow reservoir to a fast reservoir, and the fast reservoir fills up faster than it can drain.
:::

## Spiral Review

:::tip Connecting to Earlier Weeks
- **From Week 1:** Photosynthesis is the carbon cycle's main solar-powered process. Plants capture both energy *and* carbon from the system.
- **From Week 2:** Respiration is the reverse — releasing the stored chemical energy and the carbon it was bound to. Energy degrades to heat; carbon returns to the atmosphere as CO₂.
- **From Week 3:** The terrarium runs a tiny carbon cycle too. The plants pull CO₂ from the air inside, build tissue, and decomposers (in time) release it back.
- **From Week 4:** Both carbon and water are biogeochemical cycles. Same engineering model — reservoirs, flows, residence times. The atoms cycle; the energy flows through.
:::

The takeaway: "Carbon moves around between the air, plants, animals, and the ground. Right now, humans are pulling a lot of it out of deep ground and putting it into the air faster than it can move back." Skip the greenhouse-effect physics.
:::

:::tip Extend (Ages 10–12)
Look up the Keeling Curve (graph of atmospheric CO₂ at Mauna Loa, 1958–present). Notice two patterns: (1) the steady annual increase, and (2) the seasonal wiggle (CO₂ drops in northern hemisphere summer when plants photosynthesize most, rises in winter when they don't). Why does the planet "breathe" with the seasons?
