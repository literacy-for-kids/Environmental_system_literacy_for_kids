# Week 9: What Happens When Demand Exceeds Supply
*Unit 3: System Limits and Load Balancing*

Last week we defined carrying capacity. This week we look at how populations actually behave around it.

The key insight: **populations rarely sit politely at carrying capacity.** Real populations grow, overshoot, crash, recover, and sometimes oscillate around the limit. The shape of those dynamics is determined by two specific feedback loops:

- **Balancing loops** push the system toward steady state. (More prey → more predators → fewer prey → fewer predators → more prey.)
- **Amplifying loops** push the system further from steady state. (Population grows → degrades food source → habitat damaged → carrying capacity drops → next overshoot crash is worse.)

These two loops are the same architecture that drives everything from predator-prey cycles to economic booms-and-busts to climate tipping points.

---

:::info Facilitator Snapshot
- The big idea: populations behave according to feedback. Understand the feedback and you can predict the dynamics.
- Lotka-Volterra is the classic predator-prey model. Students do not need the math, just the picture: prey and predator populations oscillate, lagged in time.
- J-curve vs. S-curve growth is a clean visual way to teach the difference between unbounded and bounded growth.
- This week sets up Optional Week 1 (amplifying loops and tipping points) for groups that want to go further.
:::

## For Younger Learners (Ages 8–9)

:::info Adapting This Week
**Simplest version of the concept:** "When animals start to run out of food, the population goes up and down like a wave. Sometimes it goes up so far that the whole system crashes and a lot of animals die."

**What to shorten or skip:**
- Skip the Lotka-Volterra model entirely. Use the wolf-and-rabbit story.
- Skip the J-curve / S-curve naming. Use "growing forever" vs. "leveling off."

**Adapting the activities:**
- Draw the wolf-and-rabbit population as two wavy lines — when one is high, the other follows a little while later.

**Journal alternatives:** Drawings of populations growing, leveling off, or crashing.

**What success looks like:** The learner can describe how rabbit and wolf populations go up and down together over time.
:::

:::info For Ages 10–12
- Introduce the Lotka-Volterra equations conceptually (no calculus): rate of prey change = (prey birth rate) − (rate prey are eaten by predators). Predator change is the mirror.
- Discuss real-world data: the Hudson's Bay Company kept fur records from 1845 to 1935 — the lynx and hare populations cycled with a ~10-year period for nearly a century.
- Discuss why the cycles in some systems eventually dampen out, in others persist, and in others (like the Kaibab deer) spiral into collapse.
:::

## Guided Session 1
### The Two Feedback Shapes
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- distinguish a balancing loop from an amplifying loop
- describe predator-prey dynamics as a balancing loop
- recognize how a damaged habitat can flip a system from balancing to amplifying

---

#### Activities

**1. The Two Loop Shapes**

Draw two simple boxes on paper:

**Balancing loop:**

```
   [population grows] → [food gets scarce] →
                                            ↓
   [population shrinks] ← [hunger / death] ←
```

The loop pulls the population back toward steady state. If it's high, it gets pulled down. If it's low, it gets pulled up.

> "This is how a thermostat works. Room too cold → heater turns on → room warms up → heater turns off. The loop *resists change*."

**Amplifying loop:**

```
   [population grows past limit] → [habitat damaged] →
                                                      ↓
   [carrying capacity drops] → [overshoot is worse next time]
                                                      ↓
   [more damage] ─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each turn of the loop makes the situation more extreme, not less.

> "This is how an out-of-control fire spreads. Once it's big enough, it dries out the area around it, which lets the fire grow further. Same pattern."

---

**2. The Wolf-and-Rabbit Story**

Tell the story:

1. A field has lots of rabbits. Plenty of grass. Few wolves.
2. The rabbits eat well and have lots of baby rabbits. The rabbit population grows.
3. The wolves have lots of food (rabbits). Their population grows too — but on a delay, because wolves reproduce slower than rabbits.
4. Now there are lots of wolves. The wolves eat more rabbits than rabbits can replace.
5. The rabbit population starts to drop.
6. With fewer rabbits, the wolves start to starve. Wolf population drops.
7. With fewer wolves, surviving rabbits multiply again.
8. The cycle repeats.

If you graph it, both populations look like wavy lines. The wolf line lags behind the rabbit line — wolves are responding to a past abundance, and they always overshoot when food declines.

This is the **classic predator-prey cycle**. It's a balancing loop. Neither side can overshoot for long because the other side responds.

---

**3. The Real Data**

Show or describe the **Hudson's Bay Company lynx-and-hare data**. For 90+ years, fur trappers in Canada kept records of how many lynx and snowshoe hare pelts they collected per year. When plotted:

- Hare populations rose and fell on roughly a 10-year cycle.
- Lynx populations followed the same pattern, lagging by about 2 years.

The data is messy but the pattern is unmistakable. This is one of the longest, cleanest natural experiments confirming the predator-prey cycle in nature.

> "Two populations, locked in a balancing loop, oscillating for a century."

---

**4. When the Loop Breaks**

Now ask:

> "What happens if you remove the predators entirely?"

The balancing loop is broken. The prey population grows past carrying capacity. The habitat is degraded. The carrying capacity drops. The next crash is worse than it would have been with predators. The balancing loop has been replaced by an amplifying loop of habitat damage.

This is exactly what happened on the Kaibab Plateau (Week 8).

> "When you remove the natural feedback, you don't get a happy stable system. You get an unstable one."

## Guided Session 2
### J-Curves and S-Curves
#### Learning Goal
By the end of this session, the student can:

- recognize the J-curve shape of unbounded exponential growth
- recognize the S-curve shape of bounded logistic growth
- understand which conditions produce each shape

---

#### Activities

**1. The J-Curve**

On graph paper, draw a curve that starts near the bottom-left, stays low for a while, then accelerates upward dramatically. The result looks like the letter "J."

> "This is unbounded growth. Each new generation makes more babies than the last because nothing is limiting them. Bacteria in a fresh culture do this. Yeast in a new bottle of wine do this. Invasive species in a new environment do this. Compound interest does this too."

Key feature: the rate of growth itself is increasing. Twice as many parents make twice as many babies. The curve gets steeper as time goes on.

This is also called **exponential growth**. It is what happens when growth has no constraints.

The catch: nothing in the physical world grows exponentially forever. Eventually, something runs out.

---

**2. The S-Curve**

Now draw a different curve. It starts the same — near the bottom-left, with a slow upward curve. But as it rises, it gradually levels off, settling around a horizontal line near the top. The result looks like a stretched-out "S."

> "This is bounded growth. The population grows fast at first, then slows as it approaches its carrying capacity. At capacity, growth stops — births and deaths balance."

This is also called **logistic growth**. It is what happens when growth runs into limits.

The horizontal asymptote (the line the curve approaches but doesn't cross) is the carrying capacity, K.

---

**3. The Overshoot Curve**

Draw a third curve. It starts like an S-curve but then *overshoots* the horizontal line at K, then drops below it, then comes back up, oscillating around K with decreasing amplitude until it settles down.

> "This is overshoot-and-correct. The population had momentum — birth rates from before food got scarce — so it shot past K. Then it crashed. Then it overshot again, but less. Eventually it settled around K."

Now draw a fourth curve. It overshoots K dramatically, then crashes hard — and instead of recovering to K, it crashes well below K and stays there.

> "This is overshoot-and-collapse. The overshoot damaged the system itself, lowering K. The population can't recover to the old steady state because the old steady state is gone."

This is what happened to the deer of Kaibab and the reindeer of St. Matthew Island.

---

**4. The Shape Determines the Story**

Which shape happens depends on the system:

- **J-curve forever:** Only happens briefly in real life. Always ends with a transition to another shape.
- **S-curve:** Happens when the population can respond to limits *before* it overshoots — usually because reproduction rates are slow and feedback is fast.
- **Overshoot with damped oscillations:** Happens when there's some lag in the feedback but the system is resilient enough to recover.
- **Overshoot with collapse:** Happens when the overshoot damages the system itself — when carrying capacity drops because of the overshoot.

Climate scientists worry about the last one for planetary systems. Several Earth-system feedbacks (ice melt, permafrost thaw, forest dieback) could shift carrying capacity downward as overshoot continues. We will look at this in Optional Week 1 if the group continues to the bonus weeks.

## Independent Practice

### Goal

Observe a population curve in your own environment and continue terrarium observations.

### Activities

**1. Find a Real Curve**

Look up or ask about a real population curve in your area. Some examples:

- **Whitetail deer** in most U.S. states (often showing dramatic recoveries after near-extinction in 1900, then leveling off).
- **Bald eagles** in the U.S. (a recovery S-curve after DDT was banned in 1972).
- **An invasive species** in your area (often J-curve in the early years).
- **Honeybee colonies** (data is mixed and depends on cause).

Look at the shape. What kind of curve is it? Why?

---

**2. Draw Your Own**

Pick a hypothetical scenario and draw the population curve you'd expect:

- 10 rabbits introduced to a small island with no predators, abundant grass, plenty of water.
- A pair of swans on a small pond with limited food.
- A population of mice in a barn where the cats keep multiplying along with them.
- A bacteria culture started fresh in a petri dish.

In each case, would you expect a J-curve, S-curve, overshoot-with-recovery, or overshoot-with-collapse? Why?

---

**3. Terrarium Check**

Week 6 of terrarium observation. Open the Systems Log:

- Day count: __
- Condensation, soil, plants — any changes?
- This week, think about the plants as a population. They had abundant resources at the start. Are they still growing? Have they leveled off? Have they gotten bigger or filled the available space? Most established terrariums are in or near S-curve steady state by this point — growth has slowed, the system is in balance.

### Systems Log

> **Today's System:** Population dynamics
>
> **Sketched curves:** [J-curve, S-curve, overshoot/recovery, overshoot/collapse — draw all four with brief labels]
>
> **A real population I looked up:** ____
>
> **The shape of its curve:** ____
>
> **Terrarium update:** ____

**Sentence starters for younger learners:**
- "When there are too many ___, the population goes ___."
- "Wolf and rabbit numbers go up and down because ___."

### Reflection Questions

- Why don't bacteria take over the world? (Answer: they hit J-curve limits very quickly — food and waste.)
- A population growing along a J-curve is in what some economists call "boom mode." It feels great while it lasts. What does the system look like when the curve transitions?
- The human population is roughly 8 billion today, up from 1.6 billion in 1900. Without commenting on whether that's good or bad, what curve shape is that?

## Check for Understanding

After this week, check whether the learner can:

1. **Distinguish the two loops:** "What's the difference between a balancing loop and an amplifying loop?" (Looking for: one pulls back toward steady state; the other pushes further away.)
2. **Read a curve:** Show a J-curve and ask what's happening. (Looking for: rapid, unbounded growth.)
3. **Apply the predator-prey idea:** "Why would removing all the wolves from a forest cause the deer population to crash?" (Looking for: overshoot of carrying capacity, habitat damage, population crash.)

If the learner can do at least 2 of these, they're ready for Week 10.

## Pause and Notice

:::note What Matters Here
Ask:

> "Looking at the four curve shapes — which one would you want a system you care about to be on?"

Most students pick the S-curve. It's stable and the population doesn't collapse.

> "But here's the thing: a system can't choose its own curve. The curve emerges from the feedback structure of the system. If the structure has lags and no damping, overshoot is almost guaranteed."

This is the deep insight of feedback dynamics: **the shape of the future is encoded in the structure of the present**. You can predict roughly what will happen by mapping the feedback loops.

**This week's takeaway:** Populations follow feedback. Balancing loops produce stable curves. Amplifying loops produce runaway curves. The kind of loops a system has determines the kind of curves it draws.
:::

## Spiral Review

:::tip Connecting to Earlier Weeks
- **From Week 8 (carrying capacity):** Carrying capacity sets the horizontal line on the S-curve. Above it, the system is in overshoot. Below it, in growth phase.
- **From Week 6 (nitrogen):** Algae blooms are J-curves followed by crashes — bacterial decomposition consumes oxygen, fish die, the system tips over.
- **From Week 5 (carbon):** Atmospheric CO₂ has been on a J-curve since 1800. What kind of feedback structure would convert that J into an S, with steady state somewhere above pre-industrial but below disaster? (Answer: a balancing loop where rising CO₂ drives action that limits further input. Such loops can be technological, economic, or political.)
:::
